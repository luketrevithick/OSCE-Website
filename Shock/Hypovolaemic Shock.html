Simulated Patient Scenario

Hypovolaemic Shock (Adult Trauma)

Scenario Details

Scenario: Adult patient with signs of shock following a fall
Simulated Patient: 45-year-old Male Mannequin or Actor
Actor/Actress: Bystander/Witness (provides limited initial info)

MDT Information

  • Urgent response - Fall from height
  • 45-year-old male, fell approx 3-4 meters from ladder
  • Patient confused, pale
  • Patient breathing rapidly
  • Bystander present

On Arrival

Patient is lying supine on grass at the base of a ladder leaning against a house. He is moaning, restless, and pale. A bystander states they saw him fall from the ladder while cleaning gutters. No obvious external haemorrhage noted initially.

Physical Examination (Secondary Survey - Trauma Focused)

  • **Head/Neck:** No obvious trauma, check C-spine tenderness (difficult to assess reliably).
  • **Chest:** Rapid shallow breathing, equal air entry bilaterally on auscultation. Tenderness over left lower ribs on palpation. No crepitus or flail segment noted.
  • **Abdomen:** Distended, tense, and generally tender on palpation, particularly left upper quadrant and flank. Guarding present. Bruising may start to appear over flank/abdomen (Grey Turner's/Cullen's sign - likely develops later).
  • **Pelvis:** Appears stable on gentle compression/palpation (avoid 'springing').
  • **Limbs:** Check for fractures/deformity. No gross deformity noted in limbs. Peripheral pulses weak.
  • **Back:** Log roll required (maintain C-spine precautions) to assess spine and back for tenderness or deformity.
  • **(Consider FAST scan if available and trained)**

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise signs of decompensated hypovolaemic shock secondary to suspected intra-abdominal and/or retroperitoneal haemorrhage (e.g., splenic/hepatic/renal injury, pelvic fracture bleed) post-trauma. **Time critical.**
  • Primary survey (ABCDE), manage immediate life threats.
  • Airway management: Consider basic adjuncts (OPA/NPA) if tolerated, monitor closely for deterioration requiring advanced airway (RSI). High risk of aspiration.
  • Breathing: High-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask aiming SpO2 >94%. Assist ventilations if inadequate.
  • Circulation:
    • Attempt haemorrhage control (limited pre-hospital options for internal bleeding). Pelvic binder if suspected pelvic involvement.
    • Establish large bore IV access x 2, preferably antecubital fossae. Consider IO access if IV difficult/delayed.
    • **Aggressive fluid resuscitation:** Initial bolus of warmed crystalloid (e.g., 1000ml Sodium Chloride 0.9% or Hartmann's solution) rapidly. Reassess response (BP, HR, mental status). Titrate further fluid to maintain radial pulse or target BP (e.g., systolic 80-90 mmHg - permissive hypotension until definitive haemorrhage control, follow local guidelines). Avoid excessive fluid resuscitation causing haemodilution/hypothermia.
    • Consider Tranexamic Acid (TXA) according to local trauma guidelines (e.g., 1g IV over 10 mins within 3 hours of injury).
    • Keep patient warm (blankets, warmed fluids) to prevent/treat hypothermia (part of lethal triad).
  • Disability: Maintain C-spine precautions. Reassess GCS frequently. Manage pain cautiously with IV analgesia (e.g., small titrated doses of Fentanyl) once BP allows, monitor respiratory status.
  • Exposure: Expose fully to assess injuries, then cover to prevent heat loss.
  • Minimise on-scene time. Package patient safely (e.g., scoop stretcher, vacuum mattress) maintaining spinal alignment.
  • **Pre-notify receiving hospital (Trauma Alert):** Provide age, mechanism, suspected injuries (intra-abdominal bleed), GCS, vital signs, treatments given (fluids, TXA), ETA. Specify need for trauma team activation / potential need for massive transfusion protocol.
  • Rapid transport to the nearest appropriate Major Trauma Centre. Continuous monitoring en route.
