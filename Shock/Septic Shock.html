Simulated Patient Scenario

Septic Shock (Elderly Patient)

Scenario Details

Scenario: Elderly patient with altered mental status, suspected sepsis progressing to shock.
Simulated Patient: 82-year-old Female Mannequin or Actor
Actor/Actress: Home Care Worker / Family Member (provides collateral).

MDT Information

  • Urgent response - Unwell Elder / Altered Consciousness
  • 82-year-old female, confused, lethargic
  • Possible infection / Sepsis
  • Patient not responding normally
  • Care worker present

On Arrival

Paramedics arrive at a residential home. A home care worker meets them, reporting the patient has become increasingly confused and difficult to rouse over the past 12 hours. She has a known history of recurrent UTIs and had urinary symptoms starting 3 days ago (dysuria, frequency). Today she refused food/fluids and has been very sleepy. Patient is found lying in bed, appears flushed, breathing rapidly, and only responds minimally to voice.

Physical Examination (Focused)

  • **General:** Lethargic, acutely unwell appearance, flushed.
  • **Head/Neck:** Dry mucous membranes. No neck stiffness.
  • **Chest:** Tachypnoeic, clear lung sounds bilaterally. Tachycardic.
  • **Abdomen:** Soft. May have mild suprapubic tenderness on palpation. No guarding or rebound.
  • **Skin:** Warm, flushed, dry. No obvious rashes or wounds. Check for pressure areas.
  • **Neurology:** GCS 9, moving all limbs spontaneously/to pain. No focal deficits apparent.
  • **Sepsis Screening:** Meets criteria based on suspected infection (UTI), fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea, altered mental status, hypotension. (e.g., qSOFA score >= 2).

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise Septic Shock (Sepsis with persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg or lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation - prehospital recognition based on Sepsis + Hypotension + Altered LOC/Tachypnoea). Likely source UTI. **Time critical emergency.**
  • Primary survey (ABCDE), manage immediate life threats.
  • **Oxygen:** High-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask or nasal cannula to maintain SpO2 ≥94%.
  • **IV Access:** Establish large bore IV access x 2 if possible.
  • **Fluid Resuscitation:** **Administer IV crystalloid fluid bolus rapidly** (e.g., 500ml - 1000ml Sodium Chloride 0.9% or Hartmann's solution over 15-30 mins). **Use caution** given history of heart failure - monitor for signs of fluid overload (listen to lung sounds frequently). Titrate further fluids based on response (BP, HR, mental status, urine output if measurable) and signs of overload, aiming for MAP ≥ 65 mmHg (follow local guidelines).
  • **Antibiotics:** Early administration is crucial but typically occurs in ED. Prehospital role is recognition and rapid transport. (Do not delay transport for antibiotics unless specific local protocol exists).
  • **Source Control:** Identify potential source (likely UTI here). Requires hospital investigation/treatment.
  • **Monitoring:** Continuous monitoring of vital signs (HR, BP, RR, SpO2, Temp, GCS), EtCO2 if available. Monitor ECG. Check BGL.
  • **Vasopressors:** Consider if hypotensive despite initial fluid resuscitation (usually ED management, but some services may initiate prehospital per protocol).
  • Keep patient warm if temperature starts to drop or if becoming peripherally cool (later stages of shock).
  • Transport patient urgently to hospital.
  • **Pre-notify:** **Sepsis Alert** to receiving hospital. Provide age, suspected source (UTI), vital signs (confirming hypotension, tachycardia, fever, tachypnoea, ALOC), relevant PMHx (HF, AF, dementia), treatments given (O2, fluids - volume and response), ETA. Request sepsis pathway activation.
  • Documentation: Detailed record of assessment findings (including sepsis criteria met), vital signs trends, interventions (fluids, O2), response to treatment, communication with family/carer.
