Simulated Patient Scenario

Neurogenic Shock (Spinal Cord Injury)

Scenario Details

Scenario: Young adult male post-diving accident with signs of shock and neurological deficit.
Simulated Patient: 22-year-old Male Mannequin or Actor
Actor/Actress: Bystander/Friend (provides mechanism details).

MDT Information

  • Urgent response - Diving Accident / Trauma
  • 22-year-old male, dived into shallow water
  • Patient complaining of neck pain, unable to move legs
  • Patient conscious but parameters abnormal
  • Friends present

On Arrival

Paramedics arrive at a riverside location. The patient is lying supine on the river bank, having been pulled from the water by friends. Manual C-spine immobilisation is being held by a friend. Patient is awake but appears distressed, stating he hit his head on the bottom when diving in and now can't feel or move his legs and has neck pain. His skin below the chest appears warm and dry, contrasting with cool, pale skin above.

Physical Examination (Focused Neuro/Spinal)

  • **Neurology:**
    • **Motor:** Normal power in upper limbs (C5-T1 intact). Absent voluntary movement in lower limbs (below T1).
    • **Sensory:** Intact sensation in upper limbs and upper chest. Absent sensation to light touch and pinprick below nipple line (approx T4 dermatome).
    • **Reflexes:** May be initially absent below level of injury (spinal shock).
    • **Priapism:** Check for sustained erection (sign of spinal cord injury).
  • **Spine:** Palpate cervical spine gently for tenderness or deformity (midline tenderness likely present). Maintain immobilisation.
  • **Skin:** Confirm demarcation between warm/dry skin below injury level and cool/pale skin above.
  • Differentiate from Hypovolaemic Shock: Key difference is bradycardia (or normal HR) and warm peripheries below injury level in neurogenic shock, versus tachycardia and cool peripheries in hypovolaemic shock. Must also rule out occult haemorrhage.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise Neurogenic Shock secondary to acute traumatic spinal cord injury (likely cervical/high thoracic). **Time critical.**
  • **Spinal Motion Restriction:** Maintain full spinal precautions throughout (manual immobilisation initially, then appropriate device e.g., vacuum mattress/scoop + collar + head blocks).
  • Primary survey (ABCDE), manage immediate life threats.
  • **Airway:** Maintain patent airway with appropriate spinal precautions (e.g., jaw thrust). Monitor for respiratory fatigue.
  • **Breathing:** High-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask or nasal cannula to maintain SpO2 ≥94%. Monitor respiratory rate, depth, and effort closely. Be prepared to assist ventilations if hypoventilation develops.
  • **Circulation:**
    • Establish IV access x 2 if possible.
    • **Fluid Therapy:** Administer IV crystalloid fluids **cautiously**. Neurogenic shock involves vasodilation, but patients are often not volume depleted initially. Give small boluses (e.g., 250-500ml) and reassess BP frequently. Aim for MAP improvement (target MAP 85-90 mmHg often cited for spinal cord perfusion, follow local guidelines). Avoid aggressive fluid loading which can cause overload/pulmonary oedema.
    • **Vasopressors:** Often required early. If hypotension persists despite cautious fluid challenge, initiate vasopressor infusion (e.g., Noradrenaline/Norepinephrine or Metaraminol) according to local protocols, titrating to target MAP. Aim to restore vascular tone.
    • **Bradycardia:** If severe symptomatic bradycardia (e.g., HR < 40-50 with worsening hypotension), consider Atropine per local guidelines.
  • **Disability:** Assess and document neurological level accurately. Manage neck pain appropriately (analgesia - consider IV Paracetamol, cautious opioids).
  • **Exposure/Environment:** Remove wet clothing, cover patient with blankets (including space blanket), actively prevent heat loss. Monitor core temperature if possible.
  • Transport urgently to a Major Trauma Centre with spinal injury capabilities.
  • **Pre-notify:** **Spinal Trauma Alert** to receiving hospital. Provide age, mechanism, neurological level (motor/sensory), vital signs (confirming hypotension AND bradycardia), treatments given (fluids - volume and response, vasopressors, atropine if used), ETA.
  • Documentation: Detailed record of mechanism, neurological assessment, vital signs trends, spinal precautions, fluid volumes, vasopressor doses, response to treatment.
