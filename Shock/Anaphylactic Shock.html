Simulated Patient Scenario

Anaphylactic Shock

Scenario Details

Scenario: Adult patient with sudden onset multi-system allergic reaction progressing to shock.
Simulated Patient: 40-year-old Female Actor/Mannequin
Actor/Actress: Patient (initially able to speak, deteriorates), Friend/Partner (provides collateral).

MDT Information

  • Urgent response - Allergic Reaction / Difficulty Breathing
  • 40-year-old female, sudden onset SOB, rash
  • Possible Anaphylaxis
  • Patient conscious but distressed
  • Friend present

On Arrival

Paramedics arrive at a cafe. The patient is sitting at a table, appearing anxious, flushed, and audibly wheezing. She is scratching at her arms where a widespread urticarial rash is visible. Her friend reports the patient ate a pastry a few minutes ago which she thought might have contained nuts (patient has a known severe nut allergy and carries an EpiPen, but hasn't used it yet). The patient complains of throat tightness and difficulty swallowing.

Physical Examination (Focused)

  • **General:** Anxious, distressed, tachypnoeic, tachycardic, hypotensive.
  • **Airway:** Hoarse voice, stridor, patient reporting throat swelling/difficulty swallowing. Angioedema of lips/tongue.
  • **Breathing:** Increased work of breathing, accessory muscle use, audible wheeze, bilateral wheeze on auscultation, low SpO2.
  • **Circulation:** Tachycardia, hypotension, delayed capillary refill.
  • **Skin:** Widespread urticaria (hives), flushing, angioedema (face, lips, eyelids).
  • **Abdomen:** May be soft but patient might report cramping.
  • **Neurology:** Initially alert, potential for deterioration in LOC.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise Anaphylaxis with signs of shock (hypotension, altered mental status) and airway/breathing compromise. **Life-threatening emergency.**
  • Primary survey (ABCDE), manage immediate life threats FIRST.
  • **Adrenaline (Epinephrine):** **Administer IMMEDIATELY.** First line treatment.
    • Route: Intramuscular (IM) injection into anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis).
    • Dose: Adult 0.5mg (e.g., 0.5ml of 1:1000 solution). Repeat every 5 minutes if no significant improvement.
    • Consider patient's own EpiPen if available while preparing own medication.
  • **Airway Management:** Position patient upright if airway patent and breathing allows. Monitor airway closely for obstruction. Prepare for advanced airway management (intubation) if obstruction worsens or LOC decreases significantly - this can be very difficult due to swelling.
  • **Oxygen:** High-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask aiming SpO2 ≥94%. Assist ventilations with BVM if respiratory effort inadequate.
  • **Circulation Support:**
    • Establish large bore IV access x 2 if possible.
    • **Aggressive IV Fluid Resuscitation:** Administer rapid bolus of isotonic crystalloid (e.g., 1000ml Sodium Chloride 0.9% or Hartmann's solution) due to vasodilation and capillary leak causing relative hypovolaemia. Repeat boluses as needed based on BP response.
    • Consider IV Adrenaline infusion if unresponsive to IM adrenaline and fluids (follow local protocols - critical care setting usually).
  • **Adjunctive Therapies (Secondary - AFTER Adrenaline):**
    • **Antihistamines:** (e.g., IV Chlorphenamine or Promethazine) - Helps with skin symptoms but does NOT treat life-threatening features.
    • **Corticosteroids:** (e.g., IV Hydrocortisone or Oral Prednisolone) - May help prevent biphasic reaction/protracted symptoms, but slow onset, not for acute life threats.
    • **Bronchodilators:** Nebulised Salbutamol (+/- Ipratropium) for persistent wheeze/bronchospasm after adrenaline.
  • Remove allergen if possible (e.g., bee sting).
  • Continuous monitoring of vital signs, respiratory status, mental status.
  • Transport urgently to hospital, pre-notifying with "Anaphylaxis Alert".
  • **Pre-notify:** Inform receiving hospital about anaphylaxis, suspected trigger, vital signs (confirming airway/breathing compromise and hypotension), **Adrenaline given (dose, route, time, response)**, fluids given, other medications, ETA.
  • Documentation: Detailed record of symptom onset, trigger, assessment findings, timings and doses of all medications (especially adrenaline), fluid volumes, vital sign trends, response to treatment.
