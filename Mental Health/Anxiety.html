On Arrival

You arrive at the university library and are directed to a quiet study area. You see a young woman sitting upright in a chair, breathing rapidly and shallowly. She appears highly distressed and tearful. A friend is sitting next to her, looking concerned and trying to comfort her. Several other students are nearby, looking on curiously. The patient clutches her chest intermittently.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Visibly anxious, distressed, tearful, restless.
  • Respiratory: Obvious tachypnoea, shallow breathing pattern. Chest clear on auscultation bilaterally. Normal work of breathing (no accessory muscle use, retractions, or nasal flaring). Able to speak in short sentences.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardic, pulses regular, BP slightly elevated.
  • Neurological: Alert and oriented (GCS 15). Complains of peripheral and perioral paraesthesia (tingling). May exhibit carpopedal spasm (hands cramping) if hyperventilation is prolonged/severe (ask patient to describe hand sensations). No focal deficits.
  • Other: Check for diaphoresis (may be present).

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Prioritize Safety & Calm Environment: Ensure scene safety. Try to move patient to a quieter area if possible, or ask onlookers to step back. Maintain a calm, reassuring demeanor.
  • Communication & Reassurance: Speak calmly and clearly. Acknowledge the patient's fear and distress ("I can see this is very frightening for you"). Explain what is happening in simple terms (related to fast breathing). Reassure them they are safe and you are there to help.
  • Rule Out Organic Causes: Perform thorough assessment (vitals, SpO2, BGL, basic neuro, auscultation) to exclude other potential causes of symptoms (e.g., asthma, PE, cardiac event, hypoglycaemia), although less likely given history and full clinical picture. ECG if significant chest pain/risk factors.
  • Breathing Coaching: Gently guide the patient to slow their breathing. Encourage slower, deeper breaths (e.g., "Try to breathe slowly with me," "Breathe in through your nose, out through your mouth"). Avoid forceful commands. Focus on slowing the rate.
  • Do NOT use paper bag rebreathing.
  • Distraction Techniques: Engage patient in simple conversation unrelated to symptoms once breathing starts to slow.
  • Supportive Care: Stay with the patient. Offer encouragement. Involve the friend for support if helpful.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Generally not indicated as SpO2 is normal/high. Can sometimes worsen anxiety.
  • Pharmacological Intervention: Generally not indicated pre-hospitally for anxiety/hyperventilation unless extremely severe and prolonged, and per local protocols (e.g., benzodiazepines - very rare).
  • Transport Decision: If symptoms resolve quickly with reassurance and breathing coaching, and no red flags identified, transport may not be mandatory BUT strongly encourage assessment by GP or mental health service. If symptoms are severe, prolonged, distressing, first presentation, or if any concerning features/uncertainty exists, transport to ED for assessment is appropriate. Discuss options with the patient.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Communicate plan with partner and patient/friend. Provide clear handover if transporting, including suspected diagnosis and interventions performed.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing hyperventilation syndrome secondary to anxiety/panic.
  • Differentiating anxiety from serious organic pathology.
  • Communication and de-escalation techniques for anxious patients.
  • Effective breathing coaching strategies.
  • Rationale for avoiding paper bag rebreathing.
  • Appropriate use of assessment tools (VSS, SpO2, EtCO2, ECG).
  • Decision-making regarding transport vs. non-transport for anxiety presentations.
  • Importance of recommending follow-up care.
