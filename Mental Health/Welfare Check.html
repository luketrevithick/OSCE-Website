Non-Urgent Welfare Check

Scenario Details

Scenario: Welfare Check - Elderly Living Alone
Simulated Patient: 82-year-old Female
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Neighbour (briefly on scene)

MDT Information

  • Non-Urgent Response - Welfare Check
  • Caller (neighbour) concerned for elderly female neighbour (82yo)
  • Hasn't seen patient for 3 days, mail piling up, curtains drawn
  • Patient lives alone, known to be frail
  • Police requested to attend for potential forced entry if no answer
  • Location: Residential house

On Arrival

You arrive at a suburban house. Mail is visibly piled up in the letterbox. The curtains are drawn. A concerned neighbour meets you and reiterates their concerns, stating the patient usually collects her mail daily. Police arrive simultaneously. After knocking loudly and calling out with no response, police force entry via the front door.

You enter the house behind police. The house is dark and cluttered. You find the patient sitting in an armchair in the dimly lit living room, wearing nightclothes. She appears frail, thin, and somewhat dishevelled but looks up as you enter.

Scene Safety Note: Allow police to ensure the immediate internal environment is safe before full entry. Be aware of potential environmental hazards (clutter, poor hygiene).

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Alert but lethargic, frail, thin. Poor personal hygiene noted.
  • Hydration Status: Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, slightly delayed CRT. Signs consistent with mild-moderate dehydration.
  • Mobility: Difficulty standing from chair even with assistance due to weakness and reported knee pain. Walker frame nearby but dusty.
  • Skin: Check pressure areas (sacrum, heels) - may have redness/early breakdown due to immobility.
  • Environment: House cluttered and dusty. Limited fresh food visible in kitchen. Multiple medication packs noted, some doses missed. Potential trip hazards.
  • Neurological: GCS 15, oriented. Moves all limbs but generally weak.
  • Cardiovascular/Respiratory: Chest clear. Heart sounds normal. No peripheral oedema.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Confirm no immediate life threats. Identify dehydration, weakness, poor mobility, and potential self-neglect / failure to cope.
  • Detailed Assessment: Focus on hydration status, nutritional status, mobility, medication compliance, and social supports. Assess home environment for safety and suitability.
  • Communication & Rapport: Build rapport with patient using empathetic and respectful communication. Assess cognitive function and capacity gently.
  • Address Immediate Needs: Offer fluids orally if able to tolerate. Assist with mobility if safe. Ensure patient is warm.
  • Consider Underlying Causes: While no acute illness reported, consider if underlying infection, medication issues, or worsening chronic conditions contributing to decline.
  • IV Access / Fluids: Consider IV access and cautious fluid administration (e.g., 250-500mL Sodium Chloride 0.9%) if moderate-severe dehydration suspected and oral intake poor, being mindful of heart failure history.
  • Social & Functional Assessment: Determine patient's ability to manage activities of daily living (ADLs), access food/fluids, manage medications, and maintain personal hygiene. Assess social support network.
  • Disposition Planning (Crucial):
    • **Transport to ED:** Strongly recommended due to functional decline, dehydration, weakness, poor mobility, missed medications, inability to cope at home, and lack of immediate support. Patient is currently unsafe at home.
    • Explain rationale for transport clearly and compassionately.
    • If patient has capacity and refuses transport: This is a high-risk situation. **Consult with clinical support/supervisor.** Explore all alternatives. Attempt to contact family (with consent). Liaise with police on scene regarding immediate safety concerns. Document refusal, capacity assessment, risks explained, and alternative plan thoroughly. Referral to GP/community services alone is unlikely sufficient for immediate safety.
  • Referrals (If Transporting): Ensure handover highlights social concerns, functional decline, and need for comprehensive geriatric assessment / social work input in ED.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Liaise with police regarding entry and initial findings. Communicate plan clearly to patient. Detailed handover to ED focusing on medical and significant social/functional issues.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing presentations of "failure to cope" or self-neglect in the elderly.
  • Importance of assessing social determinants of health (isolation, support systems).
  • Assessing hydration, nutrition, mobility, and medication management in vulnerable patients.
  • Environmental assessment for safety and clues to patient's condition.
  • Communication strategies with frail or potentially confused elderly patients.
  • Rationale for transport vs. non-transport in complex social/medical situations.
  • Managing patient refusal in high-risk non-transport scenarios (consultation, documentation).
  • Importance of interagency collaboration (police, hospital social work, community services).
  • Comprehensive handover including social and functional concerns.
