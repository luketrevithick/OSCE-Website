Physical Examination (Focused)

  • **General:** Appears stated age, emotionally distressed (labile affect - switching between tears, anger, flat affect), restless or withdrawn.
  • **Wounds:** Assess self-harm injuries (location, number, depth, length, contamination, haemostasis). Typically superficial linear cuts on forearms/wrists in this scenario, but assess thoroughly. Check for other injuries.
  • **Signs of Overdose/Intoxication:** Assess for toxidromes if substance use suspected (pupils, vital signs, neurological status).
  • **Mental State Exam (Key Focus):**
    • **Appearance/Behaviour:** Variable - tearful, agitated, withdrawn, poor eye contact or intense staring.
    • **Speech:** May be rapid, pressured, loud, or quiet and monosyllabic. Content focused on distress, relationship issues, self-criticism.
    • **Mood/Affect:** Predominantly dysphoric, anxious, angry, sense of emptiness. Affect is often intense and reactive/labile.
    • **Thought Content:** Preoccupation with relationship conflict, fear of abandonment, self-deprecating thoughts. **Assess suicidal ideation thoroughly:** thoughts, intent, plan, access to means, past attempts, protective factors. May express hopelessness.
    • **Perception:** Usually no frank psychosis, but may experience transient stress-related paranoid thoughts or dissociative symptoms (feeling unreal/detached).
    • **Cognition:** Usually grossly intact orientation, but attention/concentration impaired by distress.
    • **Insight/Judgement:** Often limited during crisis; insight into BPD diagnosis may vary. Judgement impaired regarding coping/impulsivity.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • **Prioritise Safety:** Ensure scene safety (remove potential means of harm if safe to do so). Maintain awareness of patient's potential for impulsivity.
  • **Establish Rapport & Therapeutic Communication:** Crucial for BPD. Use a calm, patient, non-judgmental, validating approach. Acknowledge the intensity of their emotional pain ("This sounds incredibly difficult for you right now"). Active listening. Be reliable and consistent. Avoid judgmental language or minimizing their distress.
  • **De-escalation:** Focus on verbal techniques. Allow patient to express feelings. Set clear, calm boundaries if behaviour becomes overly demanding or inappropriate.
  • **Risk Assessment:** Conduct thorough suicide/self-harm risk assessment. Explore ideation, intent, plans, access to means, history, and protective factors. Take all expressions of suicidal ideation seriously.
  • **Assess Capacity:** Determine if the patient has the capacity to refuse treatment/transport (often impaired during acute crisis).
  • **Manage Physical Injuries:** Clean and dress superficial wounds appropriately. Assess need for further medical treatment (e.g., sutures - unlikely for superficial cuts but possible).
  • **Address Medical Concerns:** Rule out significant overdose or other acute medical issues contributing to presentation. Check vital signs, BGL.
  • **Sedation:** Generally AVOIDED unless extreme agitation poses an immediate risk of harm that cannot be managed verbally or with presence. Focus is on de-escalation and supportive care. If sedation is necessary (rarely), use minimal dose cautiously per protocol.
  • **Involve Support Systems:** Encourage contact with therapist, family, or friends if appropriate and potentially helpful (with patient consent if possible).
  • **Transport:** Transport to Emergency Department for comprehensive mental health assessment is indicated due to self-harm and suicidal ideation. Explain the need for assessment clearly and calmly.
  • **Mental Health Act:** Consider need for involuntary transport/assessment under relevant mental health legislation if patient lacks capacity and refuses transport despite being high risk. This often requires specific procedures/personnel (e.g., police, authorised mental health practitioners) depending on jurisdiction.
  • **Pre-notify:** Provide clear handover to ED including diagnosis of BPD (if known), presenting problem (self-harm, SI, trigger), risk assessment findings, mental state exam key points, and management provided. Emphasise the need for mental health team assessment.
  • Documentation: Detailed documentation of assessment (especially risk assessment and mental state), communication strategies, interventions, and rationale for transport.
