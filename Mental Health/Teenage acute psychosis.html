Teenager - First Presentation Acute Psychosis

Scenario Details

Scenario: First Presentation Acute Psychosis
Simulated Patient: 16-year-old
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Student Actor (Parent)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Behavioral Disturbance / Altered Mental State
  • 16yo male acting bizarrely
  • Family concerned for safety
  • Patient is awake, breathing normally, distressed

On Arrival

You arrive at a suburban home. A distressed parent meets you at the door and leads you to the living room. The patient, a 16-year-old male, is pacing the room, talking intermittently to himself. He appears dishevelled, suspicious, and fearful. The room is tidy, with no obvious hazards.

Physical Examination

  • Mental State: Appears anxious, hypervigilant. Poor eye contact. Speech may be rapid, tangential, or show poverty of content. Thought content reveals paranoid delusions (being watched/followed). Possible auditory hallucinations (responding to unseen stimuli). Poor insight into illness. Cognition difficult to assess formally but appears disoriented to situation.
  • Neurological: Gross motor function appears intact (pacing). Cranial nerves grossly intact (if assessable). No focal deficits noted.
  • General: No signs of trauma. No obvious signs of toxidrome (pupils normal, skin normal). Appears physically healthy but dishevelled.
  • Abdomen/Chest/Limbs: Unremarkable on visual inspection.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Prioritize safety: Ensure safety of patient, crew, and family. Maintain calm environment.
  • Communication: Attempt to build rapport using calm, clear, concise language. Validate distress without confirming delusions. Active listening. Engage with parent for history and support.
  • De-escalation: Use verbal techniques primarily. Avoid confrontation. Maintain safe distance.
  • Assessment: Thorough history (collateral essential). Mental state examination. Vital signs. BGL. Consider and exclude organic causes (hypoglycaemia, substance use, head injury, infection – although less likely given history).
  • Supportive Care: Reassurance. Offer supportive presence.
  • Consultation/Referral: Recognize need for urgent psychiatric assessment. Transport to Emergency Department equipped for mental health assessment. Consider early notification to receiving hospital. Involve family in decisions where appropriate.
  • Restraint/Sedation: Avoid if possible. Consider only if severe agitation poses immediate danger despite de-escalation, following local protocols (unlikely focus for basic OSCE unless specified).
  • Teamwork: Shared mental model, clear roles, communication with partner.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Communication strategies with psychotic patients.
  • Importance of collateral history.
  • Risk assessment (patient, crew, others).
  • De-escalation techniques.
  • Recognizing limitations of prehospital assessment for psychosis.
  • Appropriate destination and referral pathways.
  • Teamwork and scene management.
