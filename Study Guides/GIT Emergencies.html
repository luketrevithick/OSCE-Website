Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD)

Pathophysiology

GORD is a condition where gastric contents reflux into the oesophagus. This occurs when the lower oesophageal sphincter (LES) is compromised, either due to increased intragastric pressure or a decrease in the sphincter's tone. Chronic reflux can lead to inflammation of the oesophagus (reflux oesophagitis).

Signs, Symptoms & Differentials

Typical SymptomsAtypical SymptomsKey Differentials
Heartburn (retrosternal burning), regurgitation (sour/bitter taste), chest pain, bloating. Symptoms are often worse after meals and when lying down. Persistent cough, chronic sore throat, hoarseness, difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), asthma-like symptoms. Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) must always be considered and excluded, as cardiac chest pain can mimic GORD.

Out-of-Hospital Management

  1. Assessment: Perform a thorough history (CHAMPSS) and pain assessment (SOCRATES). Ask about triggers (meals, position) and relieving factors (antacids). A focused cardiac and GIT assessment is crucial.
  2. Rule out Red Flags: Prioritise excluding ACS. Perform a 12-lead ECG and treat as per cardiac chest pain protocols if there is any suspicion.
  3. Symptomatic Treatment: Advise the patient on positioning (e.g., sitting up). Analgesia may be considered if appropriate and ACS is excluded.
  4. Disposition: Transport is required if ACS cannot be excluded or if symptoms are severe. For known GORD, referral to a GP may be appropriate if the presentation is typical and not severe.

Upper GI Bleeding

Pathophysiology & Causes

Bleeding that originates proximal to the Ligament of Treitz (the junction between the duodenum and jejunum). It can be visible (overt) or hidden (occult).

  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Erosion of the mucosal lining of the stomach or duodenum, often caused by H. pylori infection or chronic NSAID use.
  • Oesophageal Varices: Dilated veins in the lower oesophagus caused by portal hypertension (often from liver cirrhosis). Rupture leads to massive, life-threatening haemorrhage.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear: A tear in the mucosal lining at the gastro-oesophageal junction, typically caused by forceful retching or vomiting.
  • Oesophagitis: Inflammation of the oesophagus.

Signs, Symptoms & Differentials

Key SignsAssociated SymptomsKey Differentials
Haematemesis: Vomiting of blood. Can be fresh red blood or "coffee-ground" in appearance (partially digested blood).
Melaena: Black, tarry, foul-smelling stools due to digested blood.		 Signs of hypovolaemic shock: lightheadedness, hypotension, tachycardia, altered level of consciousness (ALOC), pallor. Epigastric pain may be present with peptic ulcers. The primary goal is to identify the source and severity. Oesophageal varices are a key life-threatening differential, especially in patients with a history of alcoholism or liver disease.

Out-of-Hospital Management

  1. Safety & ABCs: A massive upper GI bleed is a life-threatening emergency. Prioritise airway management. Anticipate a difficult airway due to blood and prepare high-volume suction.
  2. Oxygenation: Provide high-flow oxygen to manage hypoxia from blood loss.
  3. Circulation: Establish large-bore IV access (x2 if possible). Commence fluid resuscitation with crystalloids to manage haemodynamic instability. Recognise that this is an uncontrollable haemorrhage.
  4. Disposition: This is a time-critical emergency. Rapid transport to an appropriate hospital with surgical and endoscopic capabilities is required. Pre-notify the receiving hospital.

Gastroenteritis

Pathophysiology

Acute inflammation of the lining of the stomach and intestines, most commonly caused by a viral infection (e.g., Norovirus, Rotavirus). It leads to fluid loss through vomiting and diarrhoea.

Signs, Symptoms & Complications

SymptomsComplication: Dehydration
Diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps/pain. Systemic symptoms like fever, chills, and body aches may be present. Mild: Thirst, dry mucous membranes.
Moderate: Lethargy, decreased skin turgor, sunken eyes/fontanelle, tachycardia.
Severe: Signs of hypovolaemic shock (marked tachycardia, poor perfusion, altered LOC).

Out-of-Hospital Management

  1. Safety: Use appropriate PPE for droplet precautions.
  2. Assessment: Perform a thorough assessment, focusing on hydration status and vital signs to identify severity.
  3. Treatment:
    • Hydration: Encourage oral fluids if tolerated. If signs of moderate/severe dehydration are present or the patient cannot tolerate oral fluids, establish IV access and administer crystalloid fluids.
    • Symptomatic Relief: Consider antiemetics for vomiting and analgesia for abdominal pain if required.
  4. Disposition: Patients with mild dehydration can often be managed at home with advice (P5 Referral). Patients with moderate to severe dehydration or who cannot tolerate oral fluids require transport to hospital.

Nausea & Vomiting (Emesis)

The Emetic Reflex

Vomiting is a complex reflex coordinated by the vomiting centre in the medulla oblongata. This centre receives inputs from:

  • The chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ), which detects emetic chemicals in the blood.
  • The vestibular system (motion sickness).
  • Vagal afferent nerves from the GIT (irritation/distension).
  • Higher cortical centres (psychogenic vomiting).

Key receptors involved include Dopamine (D₂), Serotonin (5-HT₃), Histamine (H₁), and Muscarinic (M₁).

Differentials & Red Flags

The causes of nausea and vomiting are vast. It is crucial to identify potential life-threatening causes.

Common CausesRed Flag Differentials
Gastroenteritis, pregnancy, medications, food poisoning, alcoholism. Bowel obstruction, increased intracranial pressure (e.g., meningitis, head injury), peptic ulcer disease, acute hepatobiliary disease.

Out-of-Hospital Management

  1. Assessment: Take a detailed history. Ask about the character of the vomit (Is it blood-stained? Feculent?), associated abdominal pain, and any neurological symptoms (worsening headache?).
  2. Treatment: Administer antiemetics as indicated. Treat complications such as dehydration with IV fluids.
  3. Disposition: Transport is required if red flag features are present or if the patient is unable to manage their symptoms at home.

Antiemetic Pharmacology

Ondansetron

ClassActionIndicationsContraindications
5-HT₃ Receptor Antagonist Blocks serotonin receptors both centrally (in the CTZ) and peripherally (on vagal nerve terminals). Nausea and vomiting. Prophylaxis for suspected penetrating eye injury. Known hypersensitivity, concurrent use of apomorphine, patients < 2 years of age.

Metoclopramide

ClassActionIndicationsContraindications
Dopamine (D₂) Receptor Antagonist Blocks dopamine receptors in the CTZ. Also has prokinetic effects, increasing gastric emptying. Nausea and vomiting where ondansetron is contraindicated or ineffective (in patients > 16 years). Hypersensitivity, suspected bowel obstruction, previous extrapyramidal/dystonic reactions, patients < 16 years, Parkinson's Disease.

Prochlorperazine

ClassActionIndicationsContraindications
Dopamine (D₂) Receptor Antagonist Primarily blocks D₂ receptors, but also has effects on histamine and muscarinic receptors. Nausea and vomiting specifically from vestibular causes (motion sickness, vertigo, labyrinthitis, migraine). CNS depression, patients < 21 years of age (due to higher risk of extrapyramidal side effects).

Isopropyl Alcohol (Aromatherapy)

A novel, non-invasive treatment option. The patient inhales the vapour from an alcohol swab held ~2.5 cm under the nose for up to 60 seconds. Can be used as a primary or adjunct therapy for nausea.
