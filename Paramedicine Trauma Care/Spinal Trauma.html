Mechanisms of Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

Identifying high-risk mechanisms is the first step in the "index of suspicion."

High-Risk Mechanisms

  • Fall from height (>1m or 5 stairs)
  • High-speed Motor Vehicle Collision (MVC) >100km/h
  • Rollover or Ejection from vehicle
  • Diving accidents (axial loading)
  • Bicycle or Pedestrian vs. Car

4 Common SCI Patterns

  • Flexion: Forceful forward bending (e.g., MVC lap belt).
  • Extension: Forceful backward bending (e.g., fall onto chin).
  • Axial Loading: Vertical compression (e.g., diving into shallow water).
  • Rotation: Excessive twisting force (e.g., high-impact shearing).

Clinical Presentation & Physical Assessment

Red Flags for SCI

Paralysis, paresthesia (tingling), midline spinal pain/tenderness, priapism, and "spinal shock" (hypotension with bradycardia).

Systematic Physical Assessment

  1. Inspection: Check for bruising, deformity, or wounds over the spinal column.
  2. Palpation: Gently palpate the midline for focal bony tenderness (Point Tenderness).
  3. Neurological Exam: Assess sensory and motor function in all four limbs (Grip strength, pedal flexion/extension).
  4. Pain Provocation: If the patient is alert and has no midline pain, can they actively rotate their head 45° left and right? (Canadian C-Spine Rule).

Evidence-Based Spinal Clearance

Using decision tools avoids unnecessary immobilization, which can cause pressure sores, airway compromise, and increased intracranial pressure.

NEXUS vs. Canadian C-Spine Rule (Stiell et al., 2003)

Feature NEXUS Criteria Canadian C-Spine Rule (CCR)
Requirement Clearance if ALL 5 are met: No midline pain, no intoxication, normal GCS, no neuro deficits, no distracting injury. Process: 1. Any high-risk factor? 2. Any low-risk factor? 3. Can they rotate head?
Sensitivity High (~99%) Superior (~100%)
Key Takeaway Simpler to memorize. More reliable at reducing unnecessary imaging.

Management & Extrication Principles

Self-Extrication (Nutbeam et al., 2025 Consensus)

Current evidence suggests that if a patient is clinically stable and able, self-extrication (with or without a soft collar) results in significantly less spinal movement than manual long-board extrication.

Equipment Selection

  • Soft Collars: Now preferred in many jurisdictions (e.g., NSW Ambulance) for comfort and compliance (Bruton et al., 2024).
  • NIEJ Device: Used for stable patients requiring vertical lift or complex extrication (NSW SC-03).
  • Vacuum Mattresses: Gold standard for long-distance transport and comfort.

Vehicular Extrication Reasoning

When balancing speed vs. safety:

  • Urgent: Unstable airway, major hemorrhage, or cardiac arrest (Rapid extrication required).
  • Non-Urgent: Stable patient. Prioritize minimizing spinal deviation through self-extrication or controlled roof removal.

Reflective Practice

Scenario-Based Reflection

1. Pediatrics (Age <2):

How would you immobilize a 2-year-old given their disproportionately large head? (Consider padding under the shoulders to prevent flexion of the neck).

2. Geriatrics & Kyphosis:

What are the risks of forcing an elderly patient with a "stooped" spine into a flat, supine position? (Risk of fractures, airway compromise, and intense pain). How would you use padding to "fill the gaps"?

3. Speed vs. Stability:

In a high-pressure entrapment, how do you decide between a "Rapid" and "Controlled" extrication? (Nutbeam et al. suggest that "Rapid" should be reserved only for immediate life-threats).

4. Barriers to Clearance:

Why might a paramedic choose NOT to use a clearance rule even when it is indicated? (Fear of litigation, pressure from hospital staff, or lack of confidence in assessment skills).

5. The 'Urgency' of SCI:

When does a suspected SCI become a surgical emergency? (Progressive neurological deficit). What role does a retrieval team play in these time-critical transfers?
