Femoral Fractures

1. Mid-Shaft & Traction Splinting

Mid-shaft fractures can result in 1–1.5L of blood loss into the thigh. Traction reduces pain, realigns the bone, and creates a "tamponade" effect by narrowing the diameter of the thigh.

1

Manual Traction: Maintain distal pulses and alignment.

2

Measure Splint: Using the uninjured leg (Sager or CT-6/Kendrick).

3

Ankle Hitch: Secure firmly; ensure distal perfusion checks post-application.

4

Apply Tension: 10% of body weight (max ~7kg) or until pain relief.

Contraindications for Traction:
  • Partial amputation / Near-avulsion.
  • Injuries close to the knee or ankle (distal fractures).
  • Pelvic fractures (unstable pelvis cannot take the counter-traction).

2. Neck of Femur (NOF) Immobilisation

Common in elderly "ground-level falls." Presentation: Shortened and externally rotated limb.

Standard Management:

Ref: Moore et al. (2021)

  • • Analgesia (Fentanyl or Fascia Iliaca Block where available).
  • • Support with pillows/blankets in the position of comfort.
  • • "Splinting to the uninjured limb" using broad-fold bandages.

Why NO Traction for NOF?

Traction is contraindicated for hip/NOF fractures as the injury is within the joint capsule. Pulling may cause further damage or increase intracapsular pressure.

Amputation Management

The Patient

  • ⚠️ Hemorrhage Control: Direct pressure first. If fails, apply Arterial Tourniquet immediately.
  • ⚠️ Analgesia: High doses usually required.

The Part (Preservation)

  1. Clean loosely with sterile saline (do not scrub).
  2. Wrap in saline-moistened gauze (not soaking wet).
  3. Place in a sealed plastic bag.
  4. Place the bag into a container of iced water (slurry).
NEVER PLACE THE AMPUTATED PART DIRECTLY ON ICE (Frostbite/Tissue Death).

Compartment Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Increased pressure within a non-distensible fascial compartment exceeds capillary perfusion pressure (typically >30mmHg). This leads to muscle and nerve ischemia.

The "6 Ps" Assessment:

Out of proportion

Pain

Late sign

Pulseless

Note: Distal pulses often remain present even in severe compartment syndrome.

Crush Injury & Syndrome

1. The Injury vs. The Syndrome

Crush Injury: Direct tissue damage from pressure.
Crush Syndrome: Systemic manifestations (Rhabdomyolysis) occurring after the weight is released.

The "Toxin Cloud"

Release of weight allows circulation to return to ischemic muscle, flushing toxins into the systemic circulation:

  • • Potassium ($\uparrow K^{+}$) $\rightarrow$ Dysrhythmias / Cardiac Arrest.
  • • Myoglobin $\rightarrow$ Acute Renal Failure (clogs tubules).
  • • Lactic Acid $\rightarrow$ Metabolic Acidosis.

Management (ANZCOR 9.1.7 / UK Faculty)

Pre-Release

  • • IV Fluid Loading: Essential to dilute toxins (2L Normal Saline initial bolus).
  • • 12-Lead ECG: Monitor for peaked T-waves.
  • • Tourniquets: Consider *only* if life-threatening hemorrhage or if release is imminent and fluids aren't established.

Pharmacology (Hyperkalemia Guard)

  • • Calcium Gluconate/Chloride: Stabilises myocardium.
  • • Sodium Bicarbonate: Treats acidosis, shifts $K^{+}$ intracellularly.
  • • Salbutamol: Large doses (10-20mg) shift $K^{+}$ intracellularly.
