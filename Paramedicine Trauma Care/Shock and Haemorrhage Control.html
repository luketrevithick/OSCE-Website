Pathophysiology of Shock

Cellular Metabolism and Acidosis

Shock is fundamentally a failure of the cardiovascular system to perfuse tissues adequately, leading to widespread impairment of cellular metabolism.

  • Aerobic vs. Anaerobic: Without adequate oxygen delivery ($DO_2$), cells shift from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism. This depletes ATP stores and produces lactic acid as a byproduct.
  • Ion Pump Failure: Lack of ATP causes the Na⁺/K⁺ pump to fail. Sodium and water enter the cell (causing swelling), while potassium exits.
  • Acidosis: Lactic acid accumulation lowers blood pH (metabolic acidosis). Enzymes necessary for cellular function dissociate in acidic conditions, halting cell repair and division. Acidosis also reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of hemoglobin.
  • Lysosomal Release: Acidosis disrupts lysosomal membranes, releasing enzymes that digest the cell and surrounding tissues, extending injury.

Types of Shock in Trauma

While hypovolemia is the leading cause of preventable death, trauma patients often present with mixed shock states:

  • Hypovolemic: Loss of circulating volume (hemorrhage, burns).
  • Obstructive: Physical obstruction to blood flow (Tension Pneumothorax, Cardiac Tamponade).
  • Distributive: Vasodilation/Vasoplegia (Neurogenic shock from spinal cord injury).
  • Cardiogenic: Myocardial dysfunction (Myocardial contusion, or "Takotsubo" cardiomyopathy following TBI due to catecholamine surge).

The "Trauma Diamond" of Death

The traditional "Lethal Triad" (Hypothermia, Acidosis, Coagulopathy) has been updated to the "Lethal Diamond" with the recognition of Hypocalcemia as a critical fourth component.

Component Pathophysiology Paramedic Impact (Positive/Negative)
Hypothermia
(<35°C)		 Reduces enzymatic activity required for clotting. Causes platelet dysfunction. Shifts oxyhemoglobin curve left (holding onto O2). (+) Active warming, removal of wet clothes.
(-) Exposure to environment, cold fluids.
Acidosis
(pH <7.35)		 Metabolic acidosis from lactate (anaerobic metabolism). Reduces coagulation factor activity and clot strength. (+) Optimise perfusion/oxygenation, judicious fluids.
(-) Excessive crystalloids (0.9% NaCl is acidic), hyperventilation (causes compensatory vasoconstriction).
Coagulopathy
(TIC)		 Trauma Induced Coagulopathy (TIC): Early onset (minutes). Consumption of factors, hemodilution, and fibrinolysis. (+) Hemorrhage control, TXA administration, permissive hypotension (prevents "popping the clot").
(-) Hemodilution with large volume crystalloids.
Hypocalcemia
(iCa <1.0 mmol/L)		 Calcium is Factor IV; it is required for the clotting cascade, platelet adhesion, and myocardial contractility. Citrate in blood transfusions chelates calcium. (+) Early recognition, Calcium administration (if in scope).
(-) Ignoring calcium during massive transfusion protocols.

Assessment: The "Hateful Eight"

Recognizing Decompensated Shock

Traditional vital signs (BP, HR) can be misleading. Young healthy patients compensate well; older patients on beta-blockers may not mount a tachycardia. The "Hateful Eight" are clinical signs of hypovolemia:

  1. 1. Air Hunger (Acidosis driven tachypnea)
  2. 2. Low End-Tidal CO₂ (Poor perfusion/return)
  3. 3. Sweaty/Clammy (Sympathetic drive)
  4. 4. Pallor (Peripheral vasoconstriction)
  5. 5. Venous Collapse (Low JVP/peripheral veins)
  6. 6. Abnormal Pulse (Thready/Weak)
  7. 7. Hypotension (Late sign)
  8. 8. Altered Mental Status (Agitation/Confusion)

Clinical Pitfall: A "Biphasic Response" may occur in severe hemorrhage where vagal reflexes cause a paradoxical bradycardia instead of tachycardia.

Fluid Resuscitation Strategies

Permissive Hypotension vs. TBI Targets

Permissive Hypotension: In active hemorrhage (without TBI), raising BP too high can dislodge clots ("pop the clot") and dilute clotting factors.
Target: Palpable radial pulse OR Systolic BP 80-90 mmHg.

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) Exception: Hypotension doubles mortality in TBI. The brain requires higher pressure to overcome intracranial pressure (ICP).
Target: Systolic BP >110 mmHg (or MAP >80 mmHg).

Fluid Choice: Crystalloids vs. Blood

  • Crystalloids (Saline/Hartmann's): Poor oxygen carriers. Dilute clotting factors. Acidic (Normal Saline pH ~5.5). Can worsen the "Lethal Diamond". Use sparingly.
  • Blood Products: Restore volume, oxygen capacity, and clotting factors. Risk of hypocalcemia (citrate) and hypothermia (if cold).

Haemostatic Wound Packing

Indicated for life-threatening hemorrhage not controlled by tourniquets (e.g., junctional wounds: groin, axilla, neck).

Haemostatic Agents

  • Kaolin (e.g., QuikClot): Inert mineral. Activates Factor XII (intrinsic pathway). Requires at least 3 minutes of direct pressure.
  • Chitosan (e.g., Celox-A): Derived from shellfish (but safe for allergic patients). Forms a gel-like clot on contact with blood. Independent of the body's clotting cascade (works in hypothermic/heparinized patients). Requires 5 minutes of pressure.

Procedure

  1. Expose the wound completely. Identify the precise point of bleeding.
  2. Pack gauze directly onto the bleeding vessel (bone/source). Pack tight.
  3. For narrow tracks (e.g., stab/bullet), use an applicator (Celox-A).
  4. Apply continuous, firm manual pressure for the required time (3-5 mins).
  5. Apply a pressure dressing over the top. Do not release pressure to check.

Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

Mechanism & Evidence

Mechanism: TXA is an antifibrinolytic. It inhibits the breakdown of fibrin clots by blocking the binding of plasminogen to fibrin.

CRASH-2 Trial: Showed a significant reduction in all-cause mortality if TXA is given within 3 hours of injury. No benefit (and potential harm) if given after 3 hours.

PATCH-Trauma Trial: Recent evidence suggests TXA improves survival but may not improve "favorable functional outcome" (disability-free survival) at 6 months in advanced trauma systems. However, it is still recommended for survival benefit.

Dosage: Typically 1g IV over 10 minutes (pre-hospital) followed by 1g infusion over 8 hours.

Pelvic Binders (T-POD)

Pelvic Ring Injuries

High mortality (28-50%) due to massive venous plexus bleeding or arterial injury. "Open book" fractures increase pelvic volume, allowing exsanguination.

Application

  • Indication: Significant mechanism of injury + pain/tenderness OR hemodynamic instability.
  • Placement: The binder must be centered over the Greater Trochanters of the femur (not the iliac crests).
  • Action: Reduces pelvic volume, tamponades bleeding, stabilizes fractures, reduces pain.
  • Caution: Do not log-roll if possible (use scoop stretcher) to avoid dislodging clots.

Based on Leech et al. (Shock in Trauma), Dawkins (Trauma Diamond), and current ANZCOR/QAS guidelines.
