On Arrival

You arrive at a busy university cafe. Staff direct you to a table where a young man is sitting upright, leaning slightly forward. He appears anxious and is breathing rapidly. He occasionally winces and holds his right chest wall. He looks pale.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Anxious, tachypnoeic, mild-moderate respiratory distress. Prefers sitting upright.
  • Respiratory: Tachypnoea, shallow respirations. Unilateral (Right) decreased or absent breath sounds on auscultation. Hyperresonant percussion note on Right. Trachea midline. Chest expansion may appear symmetrical or slightly reduced on the right. No subcutaneous emphysema palpable. No signs of external trauma.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia. Pulses strong and regular. BP normal. No JVD.
  • Neurological: Alert, oriented, anxious. GCS 15.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Recognize potential pneumothorax based on presentation and examination findings. High index of suspicion.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask aiming for SpO2 >94-95%.
  • Positioning: Allow patient to adopt position of comfort (usually sitting upright).
  • Assessment for Tension Pneumothorax: Continuously monitor for signs of tension pneumothorax:
    • Increasing respiratory distress
    • Worsening hypoxia despite high-flow O2
    • Hypotension
    • Tachycardia (may become bradycardic late)
    • Tracheal deviation (late and unreliable sign)
    • Jugular vein distension (JVD)
    • Cyanosis
    • Decreased level of consciousness
  • Needle Decompression (Thoracostomy): **Perform ONLY if signs of tension pneumothorax develop.** Follow local protocols for landmarking (e.g., 2nd intercostal space, mid-clavicular line OR 4th/5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line) and procedure. This is a life-saving intervention for tension pneumothorax.
  • Pain Management: Provide analgesia for pleuritic chest pain (e.g., Methoxyflurane, consider IV opioids cautiously if severe pain and haemodynamically stable).
  • Vascular Access: Establish IV access.
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG, SpO2, EtCO2 (if available), and frequent vital signs reassessment (every 5-10 mins). Closely monitor respiratory status and for signs of tension.
  • Transport: Transport patient gently, avoiding bumps, to the nearest appropriate Emergency Department. Pre-notify receiving hospital (Suspected pneumothorax, vitals, SpO2, respiratory status, any interventions).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles, effective communication. Reassure anxious patient. Clear handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing the clinical presentation of spontaneous pneumothorax.
  • Key physical examination findings (decreased breath sounds, hyperresonance).
  • Importance of differentiating simple vs. tension pneumothorax.
  • Recognizing the signs and symptoms of tension pneumothorax.
  • Indications and procedure for needle decompression.
  • Appropriate oxygen therapy and analgesia.
  • Importance of continuous monitoring and reassessment.
  • Gentle handling and transport considerations.
