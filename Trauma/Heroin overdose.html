Simulated Patient Scenario

Suspected Heroin Overdose

Scenario Details

Scenario: Young adult found unresponsive with suspected opioid overdose.
Simulated Patient: 28-year-old Male Mannequin or Actor
Actor/Actress: Friend (panicked, provides limited history)

MDT Information

  • Urgent response - Unresponsive Person / Overdose
  • 28-year-old male found unresponsive by friend
  • Possible drug overdose
  • Patient not breathing normally
  • Friend on scene

On Arrival

Paramedics arrive at a small apartment. A distressed friend directs them to the bathroom where the patient is lying slumped against the wall, unresponsive. There is drug paraphernalia (syringe, spoon, lighter) visible on the floor nearby. The patient appears cyanotic around the lips.

Physical Examination (Focused - once safe/during resuscitation)

  • **General:** Unresponsive, cyanotic, poor respiratory effort/apnoea.
  • **Airway:** Check for foreign body obstruction (vomitus).
  • **Skin:** Look for track marks (arms, legs, neck, groin), signs of skin popping/abscesses. Check for medication patches (e.g., fentanyl).
  • **Chest:** Check for signs of trauma, aspiration (crackles on auscultation later).
  • **Neurology:** Confirm GCS 3, pinpoint pupils.
  • **Environment:** Note paraphernalia present (needles - sharps safety!).

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise opioid toxidrome / overdose leading to respiratory arrest. **Time critical emergency.**
  • **Safety:** Ensure scene safety (sharps!). Use PPE.
  • **Airway Management:** Open airway (head-tilt/chin-lift or jaw thrust). Insert OPA/NPA if tolerated (unlikely initially). Suction if needed.
  • **Breathing Support:** **Immediate assisted ventilation** with Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) and high-flow oxygen (15L/min). Aim for adequate chest rise and improving SpO2. Ventilate at approx 10-12 breaths/min.
  • **Naloxone Administration:** Administer Naloxone (opioid antagonist) promptly. Route depends on access and local protocols:
    • **IM:** Often preferred initially if IV access not readily available (e.g., 0.8mg - 2mg depending on protocol). Can be repeated.
    • **IN:** Intranasal naloxone is an option if available (e.g., 1.6mg - 2mg).
    • **IV:** If IV access established, smaller titrated doses (e.g., 0.1mg - 0.4mg) can be given, titrating to adequate respiratory effort (aim RR > 10-12/min), not necessarily full consciousness.
  • **Monitor Response:** Continuously monitor respiratory rate, effort, SpO2, EtCO2, LOC, and pupils after naloxone. Repeat naloxone doses as needed if initial response is inadequate or wears off (naloxone half-life is shorter than many opioids).
  • **Circulation Support:** Monitor pulse and BP. IV access establishment. Fluids usually not required unless hypotensive or other factors present. Manage cardiac arrest according to standard ALS protocols if patient deteriorates.
  • **Consider Co-ingestions/Other Causes:** Rule out hypoglycaemia (check BGL). Consider other sedatives or causes of decreased LOC if poor response to naloxone.
  • **Post-Naloxone Care:**
    • Patient may wake up agitated or combative (withdrawal). Manage calmly.
    • Risk of vomiting/aspiration as LOC improves. Position appropriately (recovery position if appropriate and C-spine cleared).
    • Risk of re-narcosis as naloxone wears off. Continuous monitoring is essential.
  • **Transport:** All patients receiving naloxone require transport to hospital for observation and further management, due to risk of re-narcosis and potential complications (e.g., aspiration pneumonia, hypoxic brain injury, rhabdomyolysis).
  • **Pre-notify:** Inform receiving hospital about suspected opioid overdose, patient's initial state (apnoeic, GCS 3), naloxone administered (dose, route, response), current vital signs and GCS.
  • Documentation: Record details of assessment, interventions (ventilation, naloxone dose/route/time), patient response, and monitoring.
