Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Acutely unwell, lethargic, pale, profoundly weak.
  • Cardiovascular: Severe bradycardia, hypotension. Weak peripheral pulses. Check for JVD, peripheral oedema (signs of fluid overload/HF).
  • Respiratory: Bradypnoea, potentially shallow. Basal crackles may be present.
  • Neurological: Lethargic, GCS 12-13. Marked generalised muscle weakness (difficulty lifting limbs). Reflexes may be diminished.
  • Other: Assess dialysis fistula/graft if present (check for thrill/bruit). Skin may show signs of uraemia (e.g., excoriations).

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): High index of suspicion for hyperkalaemia based on history (ESRF, missed dialysis, medications) and clinical signs (bradycardia, weakness, ECG changes). Prepare for potential cardiac arrest.
  • ECG Acquisition & Interpretation: **Obtain 12-lead ECG IMMEDIATELY.** Recognize characteristic hyperkalaemia changes (peaked T waves, wide QRS, bradycardia, etc.). This is critical for diagnosis and guiding treatment.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask due to critical illness, potential hypoventilation, and shock state. Assist ventilations if necessary (BVM).
  • Vascular Access: Establish large-bore IV access urgently.
  • Cardiac Membrane Stabilisation: If ECG changes consistent with hyperkalaemia are present:
    • Administer **Calcium Chloride** or **Calcium Gluconate** IV according to local protocols. This is the priority treatment to prevent fatal arrhythmias.
  • Potassium Shift Therapy (Second line, after Calcium if indicated):
    • Administer high-dose nebulised **Salbutamol** (check local protocols for dose).
    • (Insulin/Dextrose is typically an in-hospital treatment, rarely given prehospital unless specific protocols exist).
  • Fluid Therapy: Administer IV fluids (e.g., Sodium Chloride 0.9%) cautiously. Bolus if clearly hypotensive/shocked, but be mindful of potential fluid overload in ESRF/HF patients. Titrate to BP response.
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: Consider IV Sodium Bicarbonate if prolonged resuscitation, severe acidosis suspected, and per local protocols (controversial, usually after other measures).
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG monitoring is essential. Frequent vital signs reassessment (every 5 mins or less). Monitor mental status, respiratory effort.
  • Treat Reversible Causes (Cardiac Arrest): If arrest occurs, follow standard ALS guidelines, but strongly consider hyperkalaemia as the cause and administer Calcium early.
  • Transport: **Urgent transport** to an ED capable of managing hyperkalaemia and providing emergency dialysis. Pre-notify receiving hospital (CRITICAL PATIENT: Suspected severe hyperkalaemia with ECG changes/haemodynamic instability, treatments given).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles, closed-loop communication. Anticipate deterioration. Calm communication with wife. Clear, concise handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing risk factors and clinical presentation of hyperkalaemia.
  • Critical importance of early 12-lead ECG acquisition and interpretation for hyperkalaemia.
  • Understanding the mechanism and priority of treatments: Cardiac membrane stabilisation (Calcium) vs. Potassium shift (Salbutamol).
  • Appropriate fluid management in ESRF patients with suspected hyperkalaemia.
  • Anticipating and managing cardiac arrest in hyperkalaemia.
  • Importance of pre-notification and transport to an appropriate facility (dialysis capability).
  • Teamwork and managing a critically unwell patient.
