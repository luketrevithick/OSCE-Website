On Arrival

You arrive at a university dormitory. The patient, a 22-year-old male, is lying curled up on his bed, guarding his abdomen. He appears pale and uncomfortable, grimacing with pain. He looks up as you enter but is reluctant to move.

Physical Examination (Abdomen)

  • Inspection: Abdomen flat, not distended. Patient guarding RIF. No obvious scars, masses, or bruising.
  • Auscultation: Bowel sounds present but may be slightly hypoactive.
  • Percussion: Tenderness on percussion over RIF. No generalised tympany or dullness.
  • Palpation: Maximum tenderness on palpation over McBurney's point (RIF). Voluntary guarding initially, progressing to involuntary guarding/rigidity over RIF. Possible rebound tenderness over RIF (assess gently). Rovsing's sign (pain in RIF on palpation of LIF) may be positive. No palpable masses.
  • Other Systems: Cardiovascular and Respiratory exams unremarkable apart from tachycardia.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Confirm stability. Identify likely surgical abdomen (appendicitis).
  • Differential Diagnosis: While appendicitis is suspected, consider other causes of RIF pain (e.g., mesenteric adenitis, Meckel's diverticulum, Crohn's disease, renal colic, testicular torsion in males, ovarian/gynaecological causes in females).
  • Pain Assessment: Use 0-10 scale. Assess frequently.
  • Positioning: Allow patient to adopt position of comfort (often lying still with knees flexed).
  • IV Access: Establish IV access. Consider blood sampling if within scope/protocol.
  • Fluid Therapy: Consider IV fluids (e.g., Sodium Chloride 0.9%) for potential dehydration (poor intake, vomiting, fever) and pre-operatively. Administer bolus (e.g., 500mL) if signs of dehydration, then consider maintenance rate.
  • Pain Management: Provide adequate analgesia. IV opioids (e.g., Morphine or Fentanyl titrated doses) are appropriate for moderate-severe pain.
  • Antiemetic Therapy: Administer an antiemetic (e.g., Ondansetron IV) for nausea/vomiting.
  • Nil By Mouth (NBM): Keep patient NBM in anticipation of potential surgery.
  • Monitoring: Frequent vital signs reassessment. Monitor pain score, level of guarding/rigidity, and overall clinical condition.
  • Transport Decision: Urgent transport to an Emergency Department with surgical capabilities is required for definitive diagnosis and management.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Reassure patient. Explain interventions. Provide clear pre-notification to ED (Young male, suspected appendicitis with localised RIF peritonism, vitals, analgesia/antiemetic given). Detailed handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing the classic presentation of appendicitis (migrating pain, anorexia, nausea, localised RIF signs).
  • Performing a systematic abdominal assessment (Inspect, Auscultate, Percuss, Palpate).
  • Identifying signs of peritonism (guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness).
  • Considering key differential diagnoses for acute abdominal pain.
  • Appropriate pain management strategies for acute abdominal pain.
  • Role of antiemetics and IV fluids.
  • Importance of NBM status.
  • Recognizing the need for urgent surgical review and transport to an appropriate facility.
  • Effective communication and pre-notification for suspected surgical abdomen.
