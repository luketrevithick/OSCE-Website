Scenario Details

Scenario: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
Simulated Patient: 24-year-old Male
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Student Actor (Flatmate)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Unwell Person / Altered Consciousness
  • 24-year-old male, known diabetic, found unwell by flatmate
  • Reported confusion, vomiting, rapid breathing
  • Patient is conscious but drowsy
  • Location: Apartment

On Arrival

You arrive at an apartment where a concerned flatmate meets you. They lead you to the patient's bedroom. The patient, a 24-year-old male, is lying in bed, appearing lethargic and unwell. His breathing is rapid and deep, and you notice a faint fruity odour on his breath as you approach. A vomit bucket is next to the bed. The patient looks flushed and his skin appears dry.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Acutely unwell, lethargic, dehydrated.
  • Respiratory: Kussmaul's respirations (deep, sighing pattern). Tachypnoea. Clear lungs on auscultation. Fruity (acetone) odour on breath.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia. Hypotension. Peripheral pulses may be weak. Delayed capillary refill. Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor.
  • Abdomen: May be diffusely tender on palpation. Bowel sounds may be hypoactive.
  • Neurological: Altered mental status (drowsiness, confusion). GCS 13. Moving all limbs.
  • Other: Look for potential sources of infection (e.g., respiratory symptoms, signs of UTI - though history is key here). Check injection sites.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Ensure patent airway. Assess breathing adequacy despite Kussmaul's pattern. Assess circulatory status (hypotension, tachycardia). Assess neurological status (GCS, BGL, Ketones).
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply oxygen via non-rebreather mask if SpO2 <94% or signs of significant respiratory distress/shock, otherwise nasal prongs may suffice depending on SpO2.
  • Vascular Access: Establish large-bore IV access (x2 if possible).
  • Fluid Resuscitation: **Crucial intervention.** Administer intravenous fluids (e.g., Sodium Chloride 0.9%) aggressively according to local protocols for DKA/dehydration/shock. Typically start with 1000mL bolus over 30-60 mins for adults (adjust based on patient condition, age, comorbidities, and local guidelines). Reassess frequently.
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG monitoring. Frequent vital signs reassessment (every 5-15 mins). Monitor mental status closely. Recheck BGL periodically.
  • Investigations: Obtain BGL and blood ketone level (if available).
  • Do NOT administer insulin pre-hospitally (unless specifically directed by medical control and local protocols, which is rare - fluid resuscitation is the priority).
  • Manage Nausea/Vomiting: Consider antiemetic (e.g., Ondansetron) if available and indicated.
  • Identify Precipitant: Consider potential triggers (infection, missed insulin, stress).
  • Transport: Urgent transport to an Emergency Department capable of managing DKA. Pre-notify receiving hospital (suspected DKA, vitals, BGL/ketones, fluids commenced).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles, effective communication with partner and flatmate. Calm reassurance to patient. Clear handover to ED.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing the clinical presentation of DKA (hyperglycaemia, ketones, acidosis signs like Kussmaul's breathing, dehydration).
  • Understanding the pathophysiology of DKA (insulin deficiency, counter-regulatory hormones, dehydration, acidosis).
  • Prioritizing management steps: ABCDE, IV access, aggressive fluid resuscitation.
  • Importance of BGL and ketone measurement.
  • Rationale for withholding prehospital insulin in most cases.
  • Recognizing potential DKA precipitants.
  • Importance of frequent reassessment and monitoring.
  • Effective communication and pre-notification.
