Scenario: Crush Injury - Lower Limb Entrapment
Simulated Patient: 50-year-old Male
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Co-worker/Bystander

MDT Information

  • Fire & Rescue responding for extrication
  • Location: Industrial Warehouse

On Arrival

You arrive at a large warehouse. Co-workers direct you to an aisle where heavy metal shelving has collapsed. The patient, a 50-year-old male, is lying supine on the concrete floor, pinned from the mid-thighs down by several fallen shelves laden with boxes. He is conscious, grimacing in pain, and appears anxious. Fire & Rescue crews are assessing the stability of the remaining structure and planning extrication.

Scene Safety Note: Liaise with Fire & Rescue regarding scene safety, structural stability, and extrication plan before approaching patient closely. Wear appropriate PPE (helmet, safety glasses).

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Alert, anxious, significant pain, trapped by lower limbs.
  • Lower Limbs (Distal to entrapment - if accessible): Skin may appear pale, mottled, cool. Pulses likely absent. Sensation may be reduced/absent. Motor function absent. Compartments feel tense/swollen (assessment limited by entrapment).
  • Proximal Assessment: Assess abdomen, pelvis, chest, head/neck for associated injuries. Pelvis stable. Abdomen soft. Chest clear. No obvious head/neck injury.
  • Neurological: GCS 15. Moves upper limbs well. Complains of numbness/paraesthesia in legs.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Safety & Coordination: Confirm scene safe with FRS. Liaise closely regarding extrication plan and timing. Request critical care/medical team backup if available/indicated.
  • Airway/Breathing: Apply oxygen via NRB mask. Monitor respiratory status.
  • Circulation - Pre-Extrication Management (CRITICAL):
    • IV Access: Establish large-bore IV access (x2 if possible) in upper limbs prior to extrication.
    • Fluid Resuscitation: Commence aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (Sodium Chloride 0.9%) before the crushing weight is released, if safe and feasible. Aim for 1-1.5 Litres in the hour prior to extrication (or as per local protocol). This helps mitigate systemic effects of reperfusion (crush syndrome).
    • ECG Monitoring: Apply ECG monitor before extrication and monitor continuously for signs of hyperkalaemia (peaked T waves, wide QRS, arrhythmias).
  • Pain Management: Provide aggressive IV opioid analgesia (e.g., Fentanyl or Morphine titrated doses) before extrication. Consider Ketamine if available/protocol allows for severe pain or sedation during extrication.
  • Consider Pre-Extrication Medications (Protocol Dependent):
    • Sodium Bicarbonate IV may be considered pre-release to counter acidosis (follow local guidelines).
    • Calcium Chloride/Gluconate IV should be readily available to administer immediately if ECG changes of hyperkalaemia appear, especially upon release.
    • High-dose nebulised Salbutamol may be considered pre-release for potential hyperkalaemia (protocol dependent).
  • Extrication Phase: Coordinate closely with FRS. Monitor patient closely during lifting/release. Be prepared for rapid deterioration (hypotension, arrhythmias) due to reperfusion/crush syndrome. Administer Calcium immediately if hyperkalaemic ECG changes occur. Consider further fluid bolus upon release.
  • Post-Extrication Management:
    • Reassess ABCDE rapidly.
    • Continue aggressive fluid resuscitation.
    • Continue ECG monitoring. Treat hyperkalaemia aggressively if present (Calcium, Salbutamol, Bicarbonate per protocol).
    • Immobilise potential fractures. Splint limbs.
    • Reassess neurovascular status of limbs frequently (risk of compartment syndrome).
    • Maintain body temperature (prevent hypothermia).
    • Continue analgesia as needed.
  • Transport: Urgent transport to a Major Trauma Centre with capabilities for managing crush syndrome (e.g., dialysis). Pre-notify early and comprehensively (Mechanism, entrapment time >60min, suspected crush injury/syndrome, pre-extrication treatment, post-extrication status, ECG findings, fluids given).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Essential for coordination with FRS, backup resources, and hospital. Clear documentation of times (entrapment, extrication, treatments).

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing crush injury mechanism and potential for crush syndrome (especially with entrapment > 1 hour).
  • Pathophysiology of crush syndrome (reperfusion injury, release of toxins, hyperkalaemia, AKI).
  • Importance of scene safety and interagency collaboration (FRS).
  • Critical pre-extrication management: IV access, aggressive fluid loading, ECG monitoring, analgesia.
  • Rationale and potential use of pre-extrication medications (Fluids, Bicarbonate, Salbutamol, Calcium preparedness).
  • Monitoring for and immediate management of hyperkalaemia (ECG interpretation, Calcium administration).
  • Post-extrication priorities: ongoing resuscitation, monitoring, fracture/compartment syndrome management, preventing hypothermia.
  • Importance of transport to a specialist centre and detailed pre-notification.
