Simulated Patient Scenario

Paediatric Asthma Exacerbation

Scenario Details

Scenario: Moderate-Severe Asthma Exacerbation
Simulated Patient: 8-year-old Child (Mannequin or Actor)
Actor/Actress: Parent (present on scene)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Difficulty Breathing
  • 8-year-old child, known asthmatic
  • Increased work of breathing, audible wheeze
  • Using reliever puffer with minimal effect
  • Patient is conscious and alert, distressed
  • Location: Home address

On Arrival

You arrive at a house where an anxious parent meets you at the door. They lead you to the living room where their 8-year-old child is sitting upright on the sofa, leaning forward (tripod position). The child appears anxious, is breathing rapidly, and you can hear audible wheezing. They are using accessory muscles (intercostal/subcostal retractions). A blue reliever puffer and spacer are on the table nearby.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Anxious child in respiratory distress, sitting upright (tripod), speaking in short sentences.
  • Respiratory: Tachypnoea, significant accessory muscle use (intercostal, subcostal, possibly supraclavicular retractions). Audible expiratory wheeze. Widespread wheeze on auscultation bilaterally, prolonged expiratory phase. Good air entry overall (Note: A silent chest is an ominous sign of severe obstruction).
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia. Pulses good.
  • Neurological: Alert, anxious, GCS 15.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE) & Severity Assessment: Recognize moderate-severe asthma exacerbation based on work of breathing, ability to speak, RR, HR, SpO2, auscultation findings.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply oxygen via face mask or nasal prongs to maintain SpO2 ≥ 94%. Titrate to patient comfort and SpO2.
  • Bronchodilator Therapy (First Line):
    • Administer **Salbutamol** via nebuliser (driven by oxygen) or MDI with spacer according to local paediatric protocols (e.g., Nebuliser: 2.5mg or 5mg depending on age/weight; MDI: 6-12 puffs via spacer).
    • Administer **Ipratropium Bromide** via nebuliser concurrently with Salbutamol for moderate-severe exacerbations (check local protocols for dose, e.g., 250mcg).
    • Repeat Salbutamol +/- Ipratropium frequently (e.g., every 20 minutes) based on clinical response and protocols.
  • Corticosteroids: Consider early administration of corticosteroids per local protocol (e.g., oral Prednisolone if able to tolerate, or IV/IM Hydrocortisone/Dexamethasone). Often guided by severity and transport time.
  • Positioning: Allow patient to maintain position of comfort (usually sitting upright).
  • Monitoring: Continuous SpO2 monitoring. Frequent reassessment of respiratory status (work of breathing, RR, air entry, wheeze, ability to speak), HR, and overall clinical condition, especially after bronchodilator administration.
  • Consider IV Access: Establish IV access if severe distress, requiring IV medications, or potential for deterioration.
  • Magnesium Sulfate / Adrenaline: Reserved for severe/life-threatening asthma refractory to initial treatment, administered according to specific protocols and often after consultation.
  • Transport: Transport urgently to an Emergency Department equipped for paediatric emergencies. Pre-notify receiving hospital (Paediatric asthma exacerbation - severity, vitals, SpO2, treatments given, response).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Calm, reassuring approach to child and parent. Explain treatments. Clear handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing signs and symptoms of paediatric asthma exacerbation.
  • Paediatric asthma severity assessment (mild, moderate, severe, life-threatening).
  • Appropriate use of oxygen therapy.
  • First-line bronchodilator therapy (Salbutamol, Ipratropium Bromide) - routes (neb vs MDI/spacer) and dosing considerations.
  • Role and timing of corticosteroids.
  • Importance of frequent reassessment and monitoring response to treatment.
  • Recognizing signs of deterioration and impending respiratory failure (silent chest, exhaustion, cyanosis, altered mental state).
  • Management options for severe/life-threatening asthma (Magnesium, Adrenaline).
  • Communication strategies with children and anxious parents.
