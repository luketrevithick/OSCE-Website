On Arrival

You arrive at a house where an anxious parent meets you. They lead you to the living room where the patient, an 18-year-old female, is lying pale and frail-looking on the sofa. She is awake but appears lethargic and weak. The parent states the patient fainted while trying to stand up a short while ago.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Cachectic, frail, lethargic, pale. Appears significantly underweight.
  • Cardiovascular: Marked bradycardia, hypotension. Weak peripheral pulses. Delayed capillary refill. Possible orthostatic hypotension.
  • Skin: Cool, dry, poor turgor. Fine downy hair (lanugo) may be present on face/arms/back. Peripheral cyanosis may be present if very cold/poor perfusion.
  • Neurological: GCS 15 but lethargic. Weakness. Dizziness reported.
  • Other: May have muscle wasting, brittle nails/hair, dental erosion (if purging history). Abdomen may be scaphoid.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Recognize critical illness related to anorexia complications (bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, potential hypoglycaemia, risk of electrolyte disturbance/arrhythmia).
  • ECG Acquisition & Interpretation: **Obtain 12-lead ECG early.** Look for bradycardia, prolonged QT, other T wave/QRS changes indicative of electrolyte imbalance or structural changes.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply oxygen via nasal prongs or mask if hypoxic (SpO2 < 94%) or signs of shock.
  • Glucose Management: Check BGL. If hypoglycaemic (<4.0 mmol/L), administer oral glucose if alert and able to swallow, or IV Dextrose / IM Glucagon per local protocols.
  • Temperature Management: Actively warm the patient using blankets. Prevent further heat loss.
  • Vascular Access: Establish IV access (may be difficult due to dehydration/cachexia).
  • Fluid Therapy: Administer IV fluids (e.g., Sodium Chloride 0.9%) cautiously. Start with a small bolus (e.g., 250-500mL) for hypotension/poor perfusion, reassess frequently. Avoid rapid large volumes due to risk of refeeding syndrome/fluid shifts in malnourished patients.
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG monitoring is essential due to arrhythmia risk. Frequent vital signs (including temperature). Monitor mental status. Recheck BGL after treatment if initially low.
  • Positioning: Keep patient supine initially due to hypotension/orthostasis. Move gently.
  • Communication & Rapport: Approach patient with sensitivity and empathy. Avoid focus on weight/food initially. Communicate concerns clearly to patient and parent.
  • Transport: **Urgent transport** to an Emergency Department equipped for medical stabilisation of eating disorder complications. Pre-notify receiving hospital (CRITICAL PATIENT: Suspected severe anorexia complications - bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, ?hypoglycaemia, ECG findings).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles. Gentle handling. Sensitive communication. Clear handover emphasizing medical instability and history of anorexia.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing the signs and symptoms of severe medical compromise in Anorexia Nervosa (bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, hypoglycaemia, cachexia).
  • Understanding the potential for life-threatening complications (arrhythmias, electrolyte imbalances, refeeding syndrome).
  • Importance of early ECG acquisition and interpretation in this patient group.
  • Cautious approach to IV fluid administration.
  • Management of hypothermia and hypoglycaemia.
  • Communicating sensitively with patients with eating disorders and their families.
  • Importance of transport to an appropriate facility for medical stabilisation.
  • Recognizing the limitations of prehospital assessment for underlying electrolyte disturbances.
