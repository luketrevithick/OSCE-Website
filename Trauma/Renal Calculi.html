Physical Examination (Focused Abdominal/Renal)

  • **General:** Patient is restless, unable to lie still.
  • **Abdomen:** Soft on palpation, non-distended. Mild tenderness in right lower quadrant, but maximal tenderness elicited on palpation of the right flank / Costovertebral Angle (CVA tenderness). No rebound tenderness or guarding suggesting peritonitis. Bowel sounds present.
  • **Back:** Marked tenderness over right CVA.
  • **Genitalia:** (Verbalise check if appropriate for scenario/mannequin) No obvious abnormalities. Patient reports pain radiating to right testicle.
  • **Urinalysis:** (If point-of-care testing available) May show haematuria (blood), leukocytes, or nitrites (latter two suggest infection).
  • Consider differentials: AAA (especially if older/risk factors), appendicitis, cholecystitis, pyelonephritis, testicular torsion, musculoskeletal pain.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management

  • Recognise presentation consistent with acute renal colic, likely due to renal calculus.
  • Primary survey (ABCDE).
  • **Pain Management:** This is the priority.
    • Consider NSAIDs (e.g., IV Ketorolac or Diclofenac) if not contraindicated (renal impairment, peptic ulcer disease, allergy, anticoagulation, heart failure, third trimester pregnancy). Often first-line for renal colic.
    • If NSAIDs contraindicated or ineffective, use IV opioids (e.g., titrated Fentanyl or Morphine).
    • Methoxyflurane may be used for initial rapid relief while establishing IV access/administering other analgesia.
  • **Antiemetics:** Administer IV antiemetic (e.g., Ondansetron) for nausea/vomiting.
  • **IV Access:** Establish IV access.
  • **Fluid Therapy:** Administer IV fluids (e.g., 500-1000ml Sodium Chloride 0.9%) especially if patient has been vomiting, is diaphoretic, or unable to tolerate oral fluids. Avoid fluid overload.
  • Monitor vital signs frequently, including pain score reassessment after analgesia.
  • Consider differentials and perform focused examination to rule out red flags (e.g., check for pulsatile mass for AAA).
  • Transport to hospital for further assessment, imaging (CT KUB often preferred), and management.
  • Provide reassurance to the patient.
  • Pre-notify receiving hospital with suspected diagnosis, vitals, and treatments given.
