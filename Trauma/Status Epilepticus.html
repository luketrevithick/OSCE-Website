Simulated Patient Scenario

Status Epilepticus

Scenario Details

Scenario: Status Epilepticus
Simulated Patient: 35-year-old Male (Mannequin capable of seizure simulation if possible, or Actor)
Actor/Actress: Partner/Flatmate (present on scene)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Seizure / Fitting
  • 35-year-old male, known epileptic
  • Reported continuous seizure activity for > 5 minutes
  • Partner on scene, unable to manage
  • Location: Patient's apartment

On Arrival

You arrive at an apartment where a distressed partner meets you. They lead you to the living room where the patient is lying on the floor between the sofa and coffee table (area cleared slightly by partner). The patient is actively experiencing a generalised tonic-clonic seizure: rhythmic jerking of limbs, clenched jaw, excessive salivation, cyanosis around the lips. The partner states the seizure started about 8 minutes ago and hasn't stopped.

Scene Safety Note: Ensure sufficient space around patient, protect head from injury during seizure, manage partner's distress.

Physical Examination (Post-Ictal Focus)

  • General Appearance: Initially unresponsive post-seizure, gradually becoming drowsy/confused. Diaphoretic, flushed.
  • Airway: May be noisy (stertorous) initially due to relaxed pharyngeal muscles. Excessive secretions common.
  • Breathing: Respiratory rate may be slow initially then become rapid. Assess for adequate chest excursion.
  • Neurological: Assess GCS frequently as patient recovers. Check for focal neurological deficits (e.g., Todd's paresis - temporary weakness on one side). Check pupils.
  • Head/Body: Check for injuries sustained during seizure (head laceration, tongue biting, shoulder dislocation, back injury). Check for incontinence.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Safety: Protect patient from injury during seizure. Do NOT restrain forcefully or place objects in mouth. Move hazards away.
  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Manage concurrently. Prioritise stopping the seizure.
    • Airway: Position patient (recovery position once seizure stops/safe). Suction secretions as needed. Consider NPA/OPA once seizure ceases if airway compromised.
    • Breathing: Apply high-flow oxygen via NRB mask. Assist ventilations with BVM if breathing inadequate/apneic during or after seizure.
    • Circulation: Assess pulse. Gain IV/IO access urgently.
    • Disability: Check BGL immediately. Treat hypoglycaemia if present. Administer anticonvulsant medication.
  • Anticonvulsant Therapy (Status Epilepticus > 5 mins):
    • First Line: Benzodiazepines. Administer IV Lorazepam or IV/IM/IN/PR Midazolam according to local protocols and preferred route based on access/patient status (e.g., Midazolam 10mg IM for adult).
    • Second Line: If seizure continues after first-line agent, consider second dose of benzodiazepine or second-line agent (e.g., Levetiracetam, Sodium Valproate, Phenytoin IV) per protocol, often requiring medical consultation/critical care support.
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG, SpO2, EtCO2 monitoring. Frequent vital signs and GCS reassessment. Monitor respiratory status closely, especially after benzodiazepine administration.
  • Identify & Treat Reversible Causes: Check BGL. Consider other potential causes if seizure refractory or atypical presentation (e.g., electrolyte disturbance, toxins, infection, structural lesion - history is key).
  • Post-Ictal Care: Maintain airway, provide oxygen, monitor vital signs, protect from injury, provide reassurance as patient recovers consciousness.
  • Transport Decision: Urgent transport to ED is required for all patients with status epilepticus, even if seizure terminates prehospital, for investigation and further management.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles (airway, meds, monitoring). Calm communication with partner/family. Provide clear pre-notification to ED (Status epilepticus, duration, suspected cause, treatments given, current status). Detailed handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Definition and recognition of Status Epilepticus (≥5 mins continuous seizure, or ≥2 seizures without full recovery between).
  • Prioritizing management in active seizure (Safety, ABCDEs, stopping seizure).
  • Airway and breathing management during and after seizure.
  • Importance of rapid BGL assessment.
  • Pharmacological management of status epilepticus (Benzodiazepines first-line, routes of administration - IV/IM/IN/PR).
  • Monitoring for complications (hypoxia, hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, aspiration, injury).
  • Post-ictal assessment and care.
  • Identifying potential seizure triggers and reversible causes.
  • Rationale for urgent transport even if seizure terminates.
  • Effective teamwork and communication during a critical event.
