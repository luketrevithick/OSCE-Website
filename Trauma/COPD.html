COPD Exacerbation

Scenario Details

Scenario: Exacerbation of Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
Simulated Patient: 67-year-old Male
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Difficulty Breathing
  • 67-year-old male, known COPD
  • Increased shortness of breath and cough
  • Patient sounds very breathless on phone

  • Location: Home address, patient lives alone

On Arrival

You arrive at a small house. The front door is unlocked. You find the patient, a 67-year-old male, sitting in a chair in the living room, leaning forward with hands on his knees (tripod position). He appears anxious and is using significant effort to breathe, with visible accessory muscle use. He has pursed-lip breathing and audible expiratory wheezing. His skin appears slightly grey/cyanotic around the lips. A home nebuliser machine is on a table nearby.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Anxious, severe respiratory distress, tripod position, pursed-lip breathing, speaking single words. Barrel chest appearance.
  • Respiratory: Marked accessory muscle use. Tachypnoea. Audible wheeze. Diffuse expiratory wheeze on auscultation, prolonged expiratory phase, reduced air entry. Possible basal crackles. Percussion note may be hyperresonant.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia. BP may be elevated. JVD may be present if cor pulmonale.
  • Neurological: Alert but potentially becoming drowsy/confused due to hypercapnia (GCS 14).
  • Skin: Central/peripheral cyanosis may be present.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE) & Severity Assessment: Recognize severe COPD exacerbation with hypoxia and likely hypercapnia. Risk of respiratory failure.
  • Oxygen Therapy: **Administer controlled/titrated oxygen.** Start with low-moderate flow via nasal prongs or simple face mask aiming for target SpO2 range of **88-92%** (per local guidelines for known COPD/risk of hypercapnia). Avoid high concentrations unless patient is critically hypoxic or peri-arrest. Monitor SpO2 and respiratory status closely.
  • Bronchodilator Therapy:**
    • Administer **Salbutamol** and **Ipratropium Bromide** via nebuliser (driven by air if possible, or oxygen at appropriate flow rate to maintain target SpO2). Use doses per local protocol (e.g., Salbutamol 5mg, Ipratropium 500mcg).
    • Repeat nebulisers frequently (e.g., back-to-back or every 20 mins) based on clinical response.
  • Corticosteroids: Administer early corticosteroids per local protocol (e.g., IV Hydrocortisone or oral Prednisolone if patient can tolerate/swallow safely).
  • Positioning: Allow patient to maintain position of comfort (usually sitting upright, leaning forward).
  • Monitoring: Continuous SpO2, ECG, and EtCO2 (if available - monitor trend). Frequent reassessment of respiratory status (work of breathing, RR, air entry, wheeze, conscious state), HR, BP.
  • Consider Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV - CPAP/BiPAP): Prehospital NIV may be indicated for severe exacerbations with respiratory acidosis/hypercapnia if available and crew are trained/credentialed per local protocols.
  • IV Access: Establish IV access.
  • Transport: Urgent transport to the nearest appropriate Emergency Department. Pre-notify receiving hospital (Severe COPD exacerbation, hypoxic, hypercapnic, treatments given, response, ?NIV candidate).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Calm, reassuring approach. Explain interventions. Clear handover focusing on severity, treatments, and response.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing signs/symptoms of severe COPD exacerbation.
  • Assessing severity and identifying potential respiratory failure (hypoxia, hypercapnia, exhaustion).
  • Principles of controlled oxygen therapy in COPD (target SpO2 88-92%).
  • Appropriate administration of bronchodilators (Salbutamol, Ipratropium).
  • Role and timing of corticosteroids.
  • Indications and considerations for prehospital NIV (if applicable).
  • Monitoring for deterioration (worsening WOB, drowsiness, rising EtCO2).
  • Differentiating COPD exacerbation from other causes of dyspnoea (e.g., heart failure, PE, pneumonia).
