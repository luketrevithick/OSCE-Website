Simulated Patient Scenario

Fall with Loss of Consciousness

Scenario Details

Scenario: Fall with Witnessed Loss of Consciousness (LOC)
Simulated Patient: 78-year-old Female
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Student Actor (Spouse/Witness)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Fall / Collapse
  • 78-year-old female fell at home
  • Witnessed brief loss of consciousness by spouse
  • Patient now conscious, complaining of head pain
  • Location: Home address

On Arrival

You arrive at a tidy house. The patient's spouse meets you, appearing worried. They lead you to the kitchen where the patient, a 78-year-old female, is sitting on a chair, holding a cloth to a small cut on her forehead. She appears pale and slightly confused but responds to your presence. The spouse explains she collapsed suddenly while standing near the sink.

Scene Safety Note: Assess for slip/trip hazards in the immediate area.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Alert but appears frail, slightly confused.
  • Head: ~1cm clean laceration to right forehead, minimal bleeding. No palpable step deformity or significant haematoma. No Battle's sign/raccoon eyes.
  • Neck/Spine: No obvious deformity. Midline cervical spine tenderness elicited on palpation. Full assessment limited by patient positioning/compliance initially.
  • Neurological: GCS 14 (confused). Pupils equal and reactive. Moves all limbs spontaneously, strength appears grossly equal but formal testing limited. No gross focal deficits noted.
  • Cardiovascular: Irregularly irregular pulse (AF). Heart sounds normal. No significant oedema.
  • Respiratory: Chest clear.
  • Other: Check hips/other limbs for injury related to fall. No other obvious injuries found.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Address immediate life threats. Note altered GCS.
  • Consider Syncope vs Trauma: High index of suspicion for syncope (sudden collapse without warning) leading to secondary head injury. Need to manage both possibilities.
  • Cervical Spine Motion Restriction: Apply manual in-line stabilisation initially due to fall, LOC, and C-spine tenderness. Consider appropriate immobilisation (e.g., collar, head blocks) for transport based on assessment and local protocols.
  • Head Injury Management:
    • Assess for red flags (GCS <15, vomiting, seizure, focal deficit, signs of skull #). Age >65 is a risk factor.
    • Manage forehead laceration (clean, dress).
    • Perform serial neurological observations (GCS, pupils).
  • Syncope Workup Considerations:
    • Obtain 12-lead ECG to look for underlying cardiac causes (arrhythmias, ischaemia, blocks, long QT). Note known AF.
    • Check BGL.
    • Consider orthostatic vital signs if safe and patient condition allows (likely deferred).
    • Gather detailed history about events preceding, during, and after LOC from witness.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply oxygen only if hypoxic (SpO2 < 94%).
  • IV Access: Establish IV access.
  • Pain Management: Provide analgesia for headache/laceration (e.g., Paracetamol).
  • Monitoring: Continuous ECG, SpO2 monitoring. Frequent vital signs and GCS reassessment.
  • Transport Decision: Mandatory transport to ED is required due to witnessed LOC, age >65, head strike, ongoing confusion (GCS 14), C-spine tenderness, and underlying cardiac history.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear roles. Communicate findings and plan to patient/spouse. Provide comprehensive pre-notification to ED (Elderly fall, witnessed LOC, GCS 14, C-spine tender, known AF/IHD, minor head lac). Clear handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Approach to falls in the elderly, considering both trauma and underlying medical causes.
  • Differentiating syncope from other causes of collapse/LOC.
  • Importance of witness accounts in assessing LOC events.
  • Assessment and management of minor head injury, including red flag identification and risk stratification.
  • Indications for and application of cervical spine motion restriction.
  • Importance of 12-lead ECG in syncope/collapse assessment.
  • Communication with elderly patients (potentially confused) and their families.
  • Rationale for transport decisions in patients with falls/LOC.
