Safety-I vs Safety-II in Paramedicine

Safety-I (Traditional)

  • Focus: Things that go wrong
  • Humans: Liability / source of error
  • Goal: Prevent failures
  • Methods: Find & fix, root cause analysis
  • Paramedic example: “Why did we miss that tension pneumothorax?”

Safety-II (Resilient)

  • Focus: Things that go right
  • Humans: Resource for flexibility & adaptation
  • Goal: Ensure success under varying conditions
  • Methods: Understand variability & resilience
  • Paramedic example: “How did our team adapt when the monitor failed en route?”

From Hollnagel, Wears & Braithwaite (2015) – the world has changed. Paramedicine is dynamic, unpredictable, and tightly coupled. Safety-I alone is insufficient.

The Four Resilience Abilities (Safety-II Core)

🔭
Anticipate

What might happen next?

📍
Monitor

What is happening now?

⚡
Respond

What should we do right now?

📚
Learn

What can we take forward?

Paramedic example (C&C debriefs): Crew anticipates deterioration (anticipate), monitors ETCO₂ drop (monitor), switches to BVM + PEEP (respond), then shares the adaptation in Coffee & Cases (learn).

Paramedic Scenarios – Safety-II in Action

Scenario 1: Difficult Airway in the Field

Monitor fails mid-intubation. Paramedic adapts by using BOUGIE + video laryngoscope backup plan. Outcome: FPS achieved.

Safety-II lens: How did anticipation, monitoring & response succeed?

Scenario 2: Multi-Casualty MVA

Resource overload. Team re-prioritises using triage adaptations learned from previous incidents.

Safety-II lens: Learning from what went right last time enabled success today.

Applying Safety-II Strategies in Paramedicine

1. Daily debriefs (C&C style)

Capture what went right + adaptations.

2. FRAM modelling of high-risk procedures

Visualise variability in RSI or trauma handover.

3. Build resilience margins

Extra oxygen, backup comms, flexible protocols.

Personal & Team Reflection Prompts

  • Think of your last difficult job: What adaptations did you or the crew make?
  • How often do we celebrate “what went right” in shift handover?
  • What small change could your team make this week to increase adaptability?

Functional Resonance Analysis Method (FRAM) in Paramedicine

6 Aspects of Every Function

Input – What starts it?
Output – What does it produce?
Time – Timing constraints?
Control – Rules/procedures?
Preconditions – Must be true first?
Resources – What is consumed?

Paramedic Example: RSI Function

Function: Perform Rapid Sequence Intubation

Input: Patient in respiratory failure

Variability: Monitor fails (resource variability) → crew adapts using manual checks (resonance = success)

FRAM helps visualise how everyday performance variability produces both success and risk – the essence of Safety-II.
