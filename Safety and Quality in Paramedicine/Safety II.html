Core Principles of Safety-II

Safety-II represents a shift from "preventing errors" to "ensuring success." In paramedicine, where environments are dynamic and unpredictable, resilience is found in the ability of crews to adapt to varying conditions (Hollnagel et al., 2015).

Safety-I (Traditional) Safety-II (Resilient Healthcare)
Focus: What goes wrong (errors, accidents). Focus: What goes right (daily success, adaptations).
Mechanism: Reactive; finds causes of failure. Mechanism: Proactive; understands variability.
Human Role: A source of error/liability. Human Role: A source of flexibility/resilience.
Management: Constraint and compliance. Management: Support and enhancement.

Work-as-Imagined (WAI) vs. Work-as-Done (WAD)

  • WAI: The clinical protocol, the textbook procedure, or the "ideal" quiet scene.
  • WAD: Performing an RSI in a cramped, noisy ditch while a family member is yelling.

Safety-II seeks to understand WAD to improve the system, rather than forcing WAD to perfectly match WAI.

Real-World Resilience: The Four Cornerstones

Paramedics ensure safety through four functional abilities. Consider a complex "Multicar MVA" scenario:

1. Anticipate

"What is likely to happen?"

Identifying that a trapped patient will likely deteriorate during the 20-minute extrication and preparing IV access/analgesia beforehand.

2. Monitor

"What is happening now?"

Observing the firefighter's fatigue levels or checking if the patient's breathing pattern is changing as the car roof is cut.

3. Respond

"What do we do?"

Adapting the extrication plan immediately when the patient's blood pressure drops, switching from a "slow and controlled" to a "rapid" exit.

4. Learn

"What can we take away?"

Reviewing why the communication with the fire crew was so effective on this job and sharing that strategy during a "Coffee and Cases" session (Lloyd et al., 2024).

Functional Resonance Analysis Method (FRAM)

FRAM models practice not as a linear chain, but as a set of functions with variability. It identifies how normal variations "resonate" to cause outcomes (FRAM-101).

The Six Aspects of a Function

Input: Starts the function.
Output: Result of the function.
Precondition: Must be met before start.
Resource: Needed to process input.
Control: Guides the function.
Time: Constraints or timing.

Application: If the Resource (oxygen) is low, and Time is short, the Output of "Patient Stabilization" may vary. If multiple functions vary simultaneously, the system may "resonate" into failure—or into a resilient success through crew adaptation.

Fostering a Proactive Culture

Reflective Questions for Paramedic Students:

  • Think of a time when "following the protocol" was impossible due to the environment. How did you adapt, and what Resources or Controls (FRAM aspects) did you utilize to succeed?
  • How can you use the "Safety-II" mindset to facilitate a debrief? Instead of asking "Who made the mistake?", how could you ask "How did we manage to get through that difficult scenario?"
  • Referencing Okuyama et al. (2014), how does the Monitoring cornerstone of resilience rely on a culture where junior staff feel safe to speak up?
  • In the context of the Lloyd et al. (2024) "Coffee and Cases" paper, how does organizational learning contribute to the Anticipate cornerstone for future crews?
