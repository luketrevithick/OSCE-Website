The Dual Process Model (Croskerry)

Clinical reasoning is not a single function but a dance between two distinct cognitive systems. Most diagnostic errors occur when System 1 is used inappropriately without System 2 oversight.

System 1: Intuitive

  • Fast, automatic, effortless.
  • Relies on heuristics (mental shortcuts) and pattern recognition.
  • High capacity; handles multi-tasking well.
  • Highly prone to cognitive biases.
  • Driven by "gut feeling" and affect.

System 2: Analytical

  • Slow, deliberate, effortful.
  • Relies on logic, evidence, and critical appraisal.
  • Low capacity; easily fatigued (cognitive load).
  • The "safety net" for System 1.
  • Abstract and rule-based reasoning.

High-Risk Situations for Cognitive Bias

Paramedics often operate in "Cognitive Minefields." Bias risk increases exponentially under specific conditions:

Environmental Factors

  • High Workload: Increased volume leads to "thin slicing."
  • Time Pressure: Forces reliance on fast System 1.
  • Distractions/Interruptions: Breaks System 2 concentration.
  • Noise/Chaos: Degrades cognitive bandwidth.

Individual Factors

  • Sleep Deprivation: The "fatigue tax" on System 2.
  • Cognitive Overload: Too many data points to process.
  • Affective State: Stress, anger, or over-confidence.
  • Inexperience: Lack of recognized patterns.

Affective (Emotional) Bias

Affective bias occurs when the clinician's emotional response to a patient influences their objective reasoning.

Bias Type Clinical Example Impact on Reasoning
Counter-transference Disliking a frequent caller with substance abuse issues. Dismissing physical symptoms as "behavioral."
Outcome Bias Judging a decision based on the result rather than the logic at the time. Reinforcing bad habits because the patient happened to survive.
Visceral Bias Strong positive or negative feelings toward a patient (e.g., child abuse). Over-testing or avoiding necessary physical exams.
Zebra Retreat Shying away from a rare/scary diagnosis because of the effort involved. Defaulting to a "safe" but incorrect common diagnosis.

Debiasing Strategies

Cognitive Forcing Functions

Specific conscious steps to force the brain from System 1 into System 2:

  • The "What Else?" Rule: Always ask "What else could this be?" before finishing an assessment (Differential Diagnosis).
  • Pre-Mortem Thinking: Imagine the patient has died in 2 hours; work backward to figure out what you missed.
  • Diagnostic Time-Out: Stop for 30 seconds at the back of the ambulance to review VSS and logic.
  • Checklists: Externalize System 2 to reduce cognitive load (e.g., Sepsis tools, STEMI checklists).
  • Decrease Reliance on Memory: Use apps, pocket guides, or drug cards instead of memorized doses under stress.

The Path to Debiasing

Metacognition: "Thinking about Thinking"

Metacognition is the cornerstone of reflective practice. It involves standing outside your own decision-making process to monitor, evaluate, and adjust your reasoning in real-time. "Am I being biased because this patient is shouting at me?"

Applying the Transtheoretical Model (TTM) to Debiasing

1. Pre-contemplation: The clinician is unaware they have biases or believes they are immune to them. "I'm a good paramedic; I don't let feelings get in the way."
2. Contemplation: Awareness grows. Recognizing that errors happen and biases are universal. "Maybe I should look into why I missed that MI."
3. Preparation: Planning to change. Learning about heuristics and Dual Process Theory. "I'm going to start using a 'What else' rule."
4. Action: Actively applying debiasing strategies on calls and using metacognitive prompts. "Wait, I'm thin-slicing here because I'm tired. Let's do a full set of vitals."
5. Maintenance: Debiasing becomes a habit. Continuous reflective practice and peer-review of cases.
