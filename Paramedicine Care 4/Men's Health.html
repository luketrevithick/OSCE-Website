Clinical Strategy & Assessment

National Men's Health Strategy 2020-2030

Men are often over-represented in injuries, risk-taking, and preventable chronic conditions. Paramedics encounter men at points of crisis where health engagement is traditionally low.

Five Priority Health Issues:

  • • Mental Health
  • • Chronic Conditions
  • • Sexual and Reproductive Health
  • • Injuries and Risk Taking
  • • Healthy Ageing
Sensitive History Taking:

Patients may be embarrassed or stoic. Use professional, clinical language while maintaining empathy.

  • Environment: Ensure a private space for assessment.
  • OPQRST: Specifically ask about onset during activity or sleep.
  • Associated Sx: Nausea/Vomiting (common in torsion), urinary changes, or fever.
  • Meds: Specifically ask about ED meds (PDE5 inhibitors) or medications for BPH.

Testicular Torsion

Pathophysiology & Time Sensitivity

Twisting of the spermatic cord cuts off venous drainage followed by arterial supply. Ischemia begins immediately.

  • • < 6 hours: ~90-100% salvage rate
  • • 12-24 hours: < 20% salvage rate

Clinical Presentation

  • Sudden onset severe unilateral pain.
  • Nausea and vomiting (occurs in ~90% of cases).
  • Phren’s Sign: Negative (elevation of scrotum does not relieve pain).
  • Cremasteric Reflex: Usually absent on affected side.
  • High-riding or horizontal "Bell-clapper" deformity.
NSW Ambulance Management:
  • Priority: Immediate transport to a facility with surgical (urology) capability.
  • Analgesia: High priority (IV Fentanyl/Morphine per CPG).
  • Anti-emetics: Metoclopramide or Ondansetron.
  • Keep patient Fast-Nil-By-Mouth in anticipation of surgery.

Male Sexual Physiology & Priapism

Sexual Physiology

Erection involves Parasympathetic stimulation (S2-S4) causing vasodilation via Nitric Oxide. Ejaculation involves Sympathetic (T11-L2) and Somatic control.

Paramedic Alert: Spinal Cord Injuries (SCI) above T12 can lead to Reflexogenic erections without psychological stimulus.
Priapism (Ischemic)

Painful erection lasting > 4 hours.

  • • Causes: Sickle cell, SCI, medications (PDE5 inhibitors, illicit drugs).
  • • Management: Analgesia, cold packs (caution), and urgent urology referral to avoid permanent fibrosis.

Prostate & Catheter Management

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Common in older males. The enlarged prostate compresses the urethra, leading to Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS).

Common Symptoms:

  • • Hesitancy / Weak stream
  • • Nocturia (Urgency at night)
  • • Acute Urinary Retention (The common 000 call)
Catheter Management (ECP Scope):

Extended Care Paramedics (ECP) in NSW manage catheter issues via **Protocol ECP010**.

  • Blocked IDC/SPC: Attempt to flush with sterile saline.
  • Insertion: ECPs may replace/insert IDC or Suprapubic Catheters (SPC).
  • UTI Risk: Check for cloudy/foul urine, fever, or suprapubic pain.

Prostate Cancer

1 in 6 Australian males will be diagnosed by age 85. Early stages are often asymptomatic. Paramedics may see late-stage patients with bone pain (metastases), hematuria, or obstructive symptoms similar to BPH.
