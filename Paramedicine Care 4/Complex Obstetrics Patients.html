Maternal Death in Australia

2018–2020: 50 maternal deaths (31 direct, 19 indirect).

Leading causes: Cardiovascular conditions, thromboembolism, and sepsis (8 deaths each).

“Injury to the pregnant woman… constitutes a multitrauma… there are two patients.” — Sugrue & Kolkman, 1999

Trauma in Pregnancy

Key Physiological Considerations

  • ↑ blood volume (up to 50%) → can mask hypovolaemia
  • Uterus displaces organs → altered injury patterns
  • Aortocaval compression in supine position (>20 weeks)
  • High risk of placental abruption, uterine rupture, fetomaternal haemorrhage

Paramedic Management (Protocol T23)

  • High suspicion for occult injury
  • Left lateral tilt or manual uterine displacement
  • High-flow oxygen (titrate SpO₂ 95–98%)
  • Early IV access + aggressive fluid resuscitation (mother first)
  • Minimise scene time → rapid transport
  • Do not withhold necessary interventions

MVA Note: 54% of trauma in pregnancy; 70% of major injuries from MVAs.

Thromboembolism & Pulmonary Embolism

Why High Risk in Pregnancy?

  • Hypercoagulable state (Virchow’s triad exacerbated)
  • Risk persists throughout pregnancy + puerperium
  • PE is a leading cause of maternal death

Additional Risk Factors

  • Obesity, smoking, age >35, multiparity, immobility, previous VTE, varicose veins, prolonged labour

Paramedic Approach

High index of suspicion • Oxygen • Supportive care • Rapid transport • Consider differentials (e.g., aortocaval compression).

Sepsis in Pregnancy

Infection is a leading cause of maternal death in Australia.

Risk factors: Prolonged rupture of membranes, caesarean, invasive procedures, obesity, diabetes, anaemia.

Paramedic Management

  • Early identification using qSOFA / SIRS + pregnancy-specific signs
  • High-flow oxygen
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation
  • Immediate transport with ongoing treatment en route
  • Early hospital notification

Heroin / Opioids – Special Note

Naloxone contraindicated in neonate if mother opioid-dependent (risk of severe withdrawal, seizures, pulmonary oedema).

Post Natal Depression (PND)

Affects up to 20% of women. Onset 1 month to 1 year post birth. Distinct from “baby blues”.

Common Symptoms

  • Persistent sadness, tearfulness, guilt, hopelessness
  • Irritability, anxiety, panic attacks
  • Changes in sleep/appetite, difficulty bonding
  • Thoughts of harm to self or baby

Paramedic Role

  • Build rapport and show empathy
  • Perform mental health risk assessment
  • Assess capacity and safety of mother & baby
  • Transport to appropriate facility if high risk

Always remember: In obstetric emergencies there are two patients. Prioritise maternal stabilisation to optimise fetal outcome. Based on current Australian guidelines and clinical best practice.
