Gynaecological Physiology

The Female Reproductive Cycles

The female reproductive system is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis. This involves the rhythmic fluctuation of hormones to control the Ovarian Cycle (maturation of the egg) and the Uterine Cycle (preparation of the endometrium).

  • Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH): Released by the hypothalamus, stimulates the anterior pituitary.
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Stimulates ovarian follicles to grow and secrete estrogens.
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Surge triggers ovulation and formation of the corpus luteum.
  • Estrogens: Promote follicle growth and endometrial proliferation.
  • Progesterone: Secreted by the corpus luteum; maintains the endometrium for potential pregnancy.

History Taking and Assessment

Gynaecological History Framework

In addition to a standard CHAMPSS history, gynaecological presentations require specific questioning. Ensure privacy and dignity are maintained.

Key History Components:

  • Menstrual History: Last Menstrual Period (LMP)? Regularity? Flow (pads/tampons per day, clots)? Pain (dysmenorrhoea)?
  • Obstetric History: Gravidity (total pregnancies) and Parity (births >20 weeks). Complications?
  • Sexual History: Sexually active? Pain (dyspareunia)? Recent partners? Protection/Contraception? Recent screening?
  • Symptoms: Vaginal discharge (colour, odour), bleeding (intermenstrual, post-coital), urinary symptoms (frequency, dysuria).
  • Red Flags: Post-menopausal bleeding (cancer risk), severe unilateral pain (ectopic/torsion), hemodynamic instability.

Physical Examination Limitations

Pre-hospital Rule: Do NOT perform internal pelvic examinations (digital or speculum). Do not look, touch, or examine the genitals unless there is uncontrollable haemorrhage requiring direct pressure or imminent birth.

Abdominal Exam:

  • Inspect: Distension, surgical scars (laparoscopy/pfannenstiel).
  • Palpate: Masses, tenderness (localized or generalized), peritonism (guarding/rigidity). Palpate from the umbilicus down to the pubic symphysis.

Ovarian Disorders

Ovarian Cysts

Fluid-filled sacs in or on the ovary. Most are functional and resolve spontaneously.

  • Functional Cysts: Follicle cysts (follicle doesn't release egg) or Corpus Luteum cysts.
  • Benign Cysts: Dermoid (teratomas containing hair/teeth), Cystadenomas (mucous fluid), Endometriomas ("chocolate cysts" from endometriosis).

Ruptured Cyst: Can cause sudden sharp pain. If hemorrhagic, can lead to hypovolemia.
Differentials: Appendicitis, Ectopic Pregnancy.

Ovarian Torsion

Definition: The ovary twists around its supporting ligaments, cutting off blood supply. This is a surgical emergency (time is tissue).

Risk Factors: Ovarian mass/cyst (>5cm), pregnancy, IVF.

Presentation: Sudden onset severe, unilateral pelvic pain. Nausea and vomiting are very common (70%).

Management: Analgesia, anti-emetics, transport.

Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS)

A metabolic and endocrine disorder affecting 8-13% of women. Diagnosed by Rotterdam criteria (2 of 3): Oligo/anovulation, Hyperandrogenism (hirsutism, acne), Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.

Implications: Increased risk of Type 2 Diabetes, hypertension, and endometrial cancer. In pregnancy: higher risk of pre-eclampsia and gestational diabetes.

Uterine Disorders

Condition Pathophysiology Key Symptoms
Endometriosis Endometrial-like tissue grows outside the uterus (ovaries, fallopian tubes, pelvis). This tissue bleeds during menstruation, causing inflammation and scarring (adhesions). Chronic cyclical pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, infertility, fatigue.
Adenomyosis Endometrial tissue grows into the muscle wall (myometrium) of the uterus. Heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia), severe cramping, enlarged "boggy" tender uterus.

Infections

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Infection of the upper reproductive tract (uterus, tubes, ovaries). Usually caused by STIs (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea) ascending from the vagina/cervix.

Symptoms: Bilateral lower abdominal pain, fever, abnormal discharge, deep dyspareunia, cervical motion tenderness.

Complications: Sepsis (tubo-ovarian abscess), infertility, chronic pain, ectopic pregnancy risk.

Common Vaginal Infections

  • Thrush (Candidiasis): Fungal. Thick white "cottage cheese" discharge, severe itching. Risk factors: Antibiotics, diabetes.
  • Bacterial Vaginosis (BV): Bacterial imbalance (loss of lactobacilli). Thin grey/white discharge with "fishy" odour.
  • Bartholin's Cyst/Abscess: Blockage of the Bartholin's glands at the vaginal entrance. Can become infected (abscess) causing severe unilateral pain and swelling at the introitus.

Early Pregnancy Complications

Ectopic Pregnancy

Pathophysiology: Implantation of the blastocyst outside the uterine cavity (98% in fallopian tubes). Life-threatening if ruptured.

Risk Factors: Previous ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, IVF, smoking.

Presentation:

  • Unilateral abdominal/pelvic pain.
  • Vaginal bleeding/spotting (often dark).
  • Shoulder Tip Pain: Indicates diaphragmatic irritation from free blood (hemoperitoneum).
  • Hypovolemic shock (rupture).

Miscarriage (Spontaneous Abortion)

Loss of pregnancy before 20 weeks. Most common in first trimester (genetic abnormalities).

Management of Bleeding in Early Pregnancy

  1. Hemodynamic Stability: Assess for shock. Large bore IV access. Fluid resuscitation only if signs of poor perfusion (permissive hypotension considerations).
  2. Pain Management: Analgesia is safe and indicated.
  3. Rhesus Status: Transport is required for Anti-D immunoglobulin if the mother is Rh Negative.
  4. Emotional Support: Compassionate care is essential. Acknowledge the loss.

Abortion (Termination of Pregnancy)

Medical: Mifepristone + Misoprostol (up to 9 weeks). Expect cramps/bleeding.

Surgical: D&C or Suction (up to 22+ weeks). Complications: Hemorrhage, infection, perforation, retained products.

Sexual Assault

Paramedic Considerations

  • Safety: Ensure scene safety for yourself and the patient. If the perpetrator is near, involve police.
  • Consent & Control: Restore control to the patient. Explain every move. Obtain informed consent for all assessments.
  • Evidence Preservation: Discourage showering, changing clothes, or eating/drinking if forensic examination is likely. Do not clean wounds unless necessary for bleeding control.
  • Forensic Sensitivity: Do not examine genital injuries unless life-threatening hemorrhage is present.
  • Reporting: Mandatory reporting applies for minors. For adults, respect their choice regarding police involvement.

This guide summarizes key gynaecological emergencies. Remember that pelvic pain in females of reproductive age is an ectopic pregnancy until proven otherwise.
