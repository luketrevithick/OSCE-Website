Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Appears unwell, pale, photophobic, lying still in a dark room.
  • Head/Neck: No signs of trauma. Scalp non-tender. **No neck stiffness or Kernig's/Brudzinski's sign.** Temporal arteries non-tender.
  • Neurological: GCS 15. Cranial nerves intact (assess briefly - check eye movements, facial symmetry, gag reflex if concerned). Normal strength and sensation in all limbs. No ataxia or dysmetria. Normal reflexes. Patient reports sensitivity to light and sound.
  • Cardiovascular/Respiratory: Tachycardia noted. BP may be mildly elevated. Chest clear.
  • Other: Abdomen soft, non-tender. No rash.

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey & Recognition: Recognize likely migraine based on history (previous similar episodes, aura, characteristic headache, associated symptoms).
  • Rule Out Red Flags: Systematically assess for features suggesting a more serious cause (e.g., "thunderclap" onset, fever, neck stiffness, new neurological deficit, worst headache ever, altered consciousness, seizure, trauma). Perform focused neurological exam.
  • Supportive Care & Environment: Maintain quiet, dark environment. Minimize unnecessary stimuli. Offer reassurance.
  • Pain Management:
    • Consider IV access.
    • Administer appropriate analgesia. Options may include:
      • IV Paracetamol.
      • NSAIDs (e.g., Ketorolac IV/IM if available and not contraindicated).
      • Consider IV/IM Opioids (e.g., Fentanyl, Morphine) for severe, refractory pain, used judiciously.
  • Antiemetic Therapy: Administer an antiemetic for nausea/vomiting (e.g., Ondansetron IV/IM/ODT, Prochlorperazine IV/IM - check local protocols). Often helps with headache relief too.
  • Fluid Therapy: Consider IV fluids (e.g., Sodium Chloride 0.9%) if patient is dehydrated from vomiting or unable to tolerate oral fluids.
  • Monitoring: Monitor vital signs, pain score, neurological status, and response to treatment.
  • Transport Decision: Transport to ED is appropriate given the severity, vomiting, and poor response to usual home treatment. Explain the need for further assessment and potentially different medications.
  • Teamwork & Communication: Clear communication with partner and patient. Provide clear handover to ED staff, including history of migraines, current symptoms, treatments given, and response.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing typical migraine features (unilateral, throbbing, aura, associated symptoms).
  • Importance of screening for headache red flags to exclude serious pathology.
  • Performing a focused neurological assessment.
  • Creating a supportive environment for migraine patients (low light/noise).
  • Pharmacological management options for acute migraine (analgesia, antiemetics).
  • Rationale for IV fluids in migraine management.
  • Decision-making regarding transport for severe/refractory migraine.
