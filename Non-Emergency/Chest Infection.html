Simulated Patient Scenario

Chest Infection / Pneumonia

Scenario Details

Scenario: Suspected Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)
Simulated Patient: 76-year-old Female
Actor/Actress: Student Actor (Patient), Student Actor (Daughter)

MDT Information

  • Urgent Response - Difficulty Breathing / Generally Unwell
  • 76-year-old female, increasingly unwell over past few days
  • Productive cough, feverish, short of breath
  • Daughter called, concerned about confusion
  • Location: Patient's home

On Arrival

You arrive at a neat suburban house. The patient's daughter meets you, appearing worried. She leads you to the bedroom where her mother, a 76-year-old female, is propped up in bed with several pillows. The patient appears flushed, lethargic, and is breathing rapidly and shallowly. She coughs occasionally, producing thick greenish sputum into a tissue. She looks at you but seems slow to respond and slightly confused.

Physical Examination

  • General Appearance: Elderly female, appears unwell, lethargic, flushed, mild-moderate respiratory distress.
  • Respiratory: Tachypnoeic, shallow breathing, productive cough (purulent sputum). Focal chest signs: Decreased expansion on right, dull percussion RLL, bronchial breathing and coarse crackles RLL on auscultation. Mild accessory muscle use.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia, potentially hypotensive. Skin warm centrally, cool peripheries. Delayed CRT.
  • Neurological: GCS 14 (confused). No focal deficits.
  • Hydration: Signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, poor turgor).

Assessment and Treatment

Appropriate Management Focus

  • Primary Survey (ABCDE): Recognize likely pneumonia with hypoxia, potential dehydration, and sepsis (based on RR, HR, Temp, Confusion, BP - consider NEWS2/qSOFA criteria).
  • Oxygen Therapy: Apply oxygen via nasal prongs or face mask to maintain target SpO2 â¥ 94%. Titrate flow rate accordingly.
  • Positioning: Assist patient into comfortable position (usually sitting upright).
  • Monitoring: Continuous SpO2, ECG monitoring. Frequent vital signs reassessment (including temperature). Monitor mental status closely.
  • IV Access: Establish IV access.
  • Fluid Therapy: Consider cautious IV fluid bolus (e.g., 250-500mL Sodium Chloride 0.9%) for suspected dehydration/hypotension/sepsis, reassessing frequently for response and signs of fluid overload (especially given HF history).
  • Consider Bronchodilators: If significant wheeze present (due to underlying COPD or reactive airways), administer nebulised Salbutamol +/- Ipratropium per protocol, driven by air or oxygen maintaining target SpO2.
  • Pain Management: Provide analgesia for pleuritic chest pain (e.g., Paracetamol IV/oral). Opioids generally used cautiously due to risk of respiratory depression.
  • Antibiotics: Prehospital antibiotics generally not administered unless specific sepsis protocols exist and transport time is prolonged. Focus on supportive care and rapid transport.
  • Transport Decision: Urgent transport to ED is required due to hypoxia, tachypnoea, fever, confusion, potential sepsis, and need for further investigation/treatment (antibiotics, imaging).
  • Teamwork & Communication: Communicate plan clearly to patient and daughter. Provide comprehensive pre-notification to ED (Suspected pneumonia/sepsis, vitals, SpO2, key findings, treatments given). Detailed handover.

Debrief Focus Points

  • Recognizing the clinical presentation of pneumonia in the elderly (may be atypical).
  • Key physical examination findings in pneumonia (focal crackles, bronchial breathing, dullness).
  • Assessing for and recognizing potential sepsis using vital signs and clinical criteria (e.g., NEWS2/qSOFA).
  • Appropriate oxygen therapy targets and delivery methods.
  • Rationale for cautious fluid administration in elderly patients/those with comorbidities.
  • Indications for bronchodilators in chest infections (co-existing COPD/wheeze).
  • Importance of monitoring mental status as an indicator of severity/hypoxia/sepsis.
  • Comprehensive handover including sepsis concerns.
