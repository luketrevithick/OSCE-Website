Neurology Overview

---

I. Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

  • TBI is defined as "an injury to the head/brain by external mechanical force, often resulting in impairment of consciousness and sometimes other neurological signs/symptoms."

A. Causes and Epidemiology

  • Main Causes: Falls, motor vehicle accidents, attempted suicides, interpersonal violence, sporting/recreational, and workplace accidents.
  • Demographics: "Almost 80% of TBIs occur in males."

B. Types of TBI

  • Closed/Blunt TBI (More Common): Head striking a hard surface or a rapidly moving object striking the head.
  • Penetrating TBI: Occurs when an object penetrates the skull and brain.

C. Impaired Consciousness and Assessment

  • Mechanism: Implies "diffuse brain injury to the RAS in the brainstem at any level or both cerebral hemispheres simultaneously." Local brain injury typically causes focal deficits without disrupting consciousness unless intracranial pressure (ICP) significantly increases.
  • Reticular Activating System (RAS): A "complex network of neurons that connects brainstem reticular formation... with nonspecific thalamic nuclei, the hypothalamus, and the cerebral cortex." It's "thought to have essential role in controlling arousal and awareness of oneself and the environment."
  • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS): A clinical tool for assessing level of consciousness (LOC) in brain injury. Scores are given for eye opening, motor, and verbal responses (E + M + V).
    • Best possible score: 15
    • Worst possible score: 3

D. Posturing

  • Decorticate Posturing: "Flexion of the elbows, wrists, and fingers with adduction at the shoulders. The lower limbs show extension and internal rotation at the hip, with the extension of the knees and plantar flexion of the feet."
  • Decerebrate Posturing: "Adduction and internal rotation of the shoulders, extension of the elbows, pronation of the forearms, and flexion of the wrists and fingers. The lower limbs show extension and internal rotation at the hips, with the extension of the knees and plantar flexion of the feet." This often indicates more severe brainstem damage.

E. Specific TBI Types

  • Concussion (Mild Traumatic Brain Injury - MTBI): "A blow to the head disrupts the normal functioning of the brain (temporary axonal disturbance) with some damage of neuronal cell membranes, but without gross pathologic changes."
    • Clinical Features: Confusion, disorientation, amnesia, headache, dizziness, nausea, balance issues, visual disturbances, sensitivity to light/noise, and personality/emotional changes.
    • Post-Concussion Syndrome (PCS): Symptoms lasting weeks to months.
    • Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE): Associated with "neuronal loss in the cerebral cortex and substantia nigra of the midbrain similar to Alzheimer’s disease and Parkinson’s disease."
  • Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI): "A type of traumatic brain injury that can occur from a blunt injury to the head" due to "rapidly accelerated or decelerated" forces.
    • Pathology: "Disruption and disintegration of axons in white matter without visible macroscopic brain lesions."
    • Diagnosis: Primarily clinical, with "very little imaging evidence identified on brain CT and MRI."
    • Outcome: Produces "loss of consciousness of variable depth and duration."
  • Cerebral Contusion: "Bruising of the brain tissue" from head trauma. Can occur at the "site of impact (coup injury) or on the opposite side (contrecoup injury)."
  • Cerebral Laceration: "Following a penetration of the brain by a sharp object, projectile or bony fragments after skull fracture." Can also occur without penetration in severe acceleration/deceleration injuries, affecting inferior frontal and temporal lobes.

F. Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

  • Definition: "Pressure inside the skull and thus in the brain tissue and cerebrospinal fluid."
  • Monro-Kellie Hypothesis: States that the total intracranial volume (brain, CSF, blood, lesion) is constant within the rigid skull. Normal ICP is <15mm Hg.
  • Herniation Syndromes:
    • Cingulate (Subfalcine): Cingulate gyrus shifts under the falx cerebri.
    • Central: Downward shift of diencephalon through the tentorium cerebelli.
    • Uncal (Transtentorial): Parahippocampal gyrus uncus shifts through the tentorium cerebelli notch.
    • Tonsillar: Cerebellar tonsils shift down through the foramen magnum.
  • Common Clinical Manifestations of Increased ICP:
    • Headache
    • Vomiting (often 'projectile')
    • Decreased level of consciousness (confusion, drowsiness, stupor/coma)
    • Papilloedema (swelling of optic nerve disc)
    • Pupillary dilation (one or both sides)
    • Cushing’s Triad: "Increased systolic BP, bradycardia and abnormal breathing."

II. Localized Brain Lesions: Manifestations

A. Brainstem Lesions

  • Function: Contains "all sensory and motor pathways entering and leaving the cerebral hemispheres."
  • Symptoms: Impaired consciousness, motor incoordination (ataxia, dysarthria), balance problems, cranial nerve deficits.
  • Facial Nerve Exception: Lesions of the corticobulbar input cause "paralysis/paresis of the lower half of the contralateral face below the eye (central facial palsy)." Lesions of the facial nucleus/nerve cause "ipsilateral paralysis/paresis of the whole face."

B. Cortical Lesions

Primary Sensory Cortex Lesions:

  • Somatosensory: Contralateral impairment of touch, pressure, pain, temperature, vibration, and proprioception. Localization is preserved.
  • Auditory: Loss of ability to localize sounds, not complete deafness due to bilateral projections.
  • Vestibular: Impaired conscious awareness of head position/movement.
  • Gustatory: Altered taste perception.
  • Visual: "Homonymous hemianopia which is a defect of opposite half of visual field."

Sensory Association Areas Lesions:

  • Somatosensory: Astereognosis (inability to identify objects by touch).
  • Visual: Visual agnosia (inability to visually recognize objects).
  • Auditory: Auditory agnosia (inability to recognize/interpret sounds). Left side causes inability to understand speech, right side impairs interpretation of non-speech sounds.

Primary Motor Cortex Lesions:

  • Main Feature: "Contralateral paresis/paralysis especially apparent distally."
  • Other: Dysarthria (speech production impaired, not language).

Premotor Cortex (Motor Planning Areas) Lesions:

  • Apraxia: "Inability to perform a movement or sequence of movements on command despite intact sensation, motor output and understanding of the task."

Prefrontal Cortex Lesions:

  • Loss of executive functions (goal setting, planning, monitoring).
  • Loss of divergent thinking.

Limbic Association Cortex Lesions:

  • "Personality changes (socially inappropriate and impulsive behaviour) and emotional changes (depression, apathy, euphoria, mood swings)."

Parieto-temporal-occipital (PTO) Association Cortex Lesions:

  • Left PTO: Language processing (Wernicke's area), mathematical calculation problems.
  • Right PTO: Spatial awareness. Lesions here cause "contralateral neglect, which is inability to perceive and attend to objects, or even one’s own body, in a part of space." Primarily affects the left side.

C. Language Disorders (Aphasia/Dysphasia)

  • Wernicke’s Area: "Posterior section of the superior temporal gyrus in the dominant cerebral hemisphere," involved in "comprehension of written and spoken language."
  • Broca’s Area: "Inferior frontal gyrus of the dominant hemisphere," responsible for "complex process of language generation."
  • Aphasia: Impaired comprehension and expression of language (full loss). Dysphasia is partial loss.
  • Broca’s Aphasia (Expressive/Motor/Non-fluent):
    • Caused by Broca's area lesion.
    • Understanding intact, but speech is "infrequent, difficult to start, non-fluent and effortful." "Mostly nouns and verbs with few grammatical expressions, termed ‘agrammatic’ or ‘telegraphic’ speech." Patients are "generally aware of their language difficulties which leads to frustration."
  • Wernicke’s Aphasia (Receptive/Sensory/Fluent):
    • Caused by Wernicke's area lesion.
    • Cannot understand spoken/written language. Speech is "fluent, effortless and well-articulated, often with long sentences but has many paraphrasias (word substitutions that are wrong) and neologisms (made-up words); speech is devoid of meaning and can be totally incomprehensible (‘word salad’)." Patients are "typically unaware of their mistakes."
  • Global Aphasia:
    • Lesion of both Broca's and Wernicke's areas and connections (arcuate fasciculus).
    • Severe impairment in all forms of language: "inability to use language in any form."

D. Cerebellar Lesions

  • Unilateral Lesions: Affect the "same side of the body."
  • Symptoms: Difficulties in maintaining balance, ataxia (lack of motor coordination, "persistent ‘movement errors’").
  • Gait Ataxia: "Wide-based, staggering gait, with difficulty walking in a straight line, lateral veering towards the side of lesion."
  • Limb Ataxia: Dysmetria (inability to accurately move intended distance), action/intention tremor (shaking during voluntary movement).
  • Other: Muscle hypotonia, dysarthria, gaze-evoked nystagmus (trademark finding).

III. Motor and Sensory Pathway Disorders

A. Spinal Cord Injuries (SCIs)

  • Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) Lesion Signs: "Muscle weakness/paralysis," hyperactive reflexes with positive Babinski, clonus, hypertonia (spasticity), little/no muscle atrophy.
  • Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) Lesion Signs: "Muscle paralysis/weakness," diminished or absent reflexes, absent clonus, hypotonia (flaccidity), muscle atrophy (neurogenic atrophy).

B. Corticospinal Tracts (Pyramidal Tracts)

  • Function: "Voluntary control of limbs muscles, especially fine movements."
  • Lateral Corticospinal Tract (90%): Crosses over in pyramidal decussation, innervates contralateral muscles.
  • Anterior Corticospinal Tract (10%): Some axons cross at spinal segment level, others don't, innervate ipsilateral muscles.

C. Spinal Sensory Pathways

  • Spinothalamic Tract: Transmits pain, temperature, crude touch. Crosses over in the spinal cord, ascends contralaterally.
  • Dorsal Column-Medial Lemniscus System: Transmits discriminative touch, conscious proprioception. Ascends ipsilaterally to medulla, then crosses to contralateral thalamus.
  • Brown-Sequard’s Syndrome: Hemisection of the spinal cord.
    • Ipsilateral: UMN paralysis, LMN signs (at lesion level), loss of discriminative touch/proprioception.
    • Contralateral: Loss of pain and temperature sensation (typically 2-3 segments below lesion).

D. Respiratory Function in SCIs

  • Diaphragm: Main inspiratory muscle, innervated by phrenic nerves (C3, C4, C5).
  • Cervical SCIs (C1-C2): Total paralysis of diaphragm, scalenes, intercostals, and abdominal muscles. Requires mechanical ventilation.
  • C3-C5 SCIs: Diaphragm function partially or totally impaired.
  • T1-T5 SCIs: Diaphragm works well, intercostals partially, abdominals not. Cough/sneeze impaired.
  • T6-T12 SCIs: Diaphragm and intercostals work well. Abdominals may be weak. Cough/sneeze present but weaker.

E. Bladder Function in SCIs (Neurogenic Bladder)

  • Reflexive Control: Parasympathetic centers in S2-S4.
  • Lesions Above Sacral Cord (UMN Bladder): Interrupt ascending sensory and descending control. Intact sacral reflex leads to "hypertonic, hyper-reflexive bladder and increased tone of the external sphincter (detrusor-external sphincter dyssynergia)." Results in "reduced bladder filling capacity (spastic bladder), high pressures... but reflexive uncontrolled emptying can occur automatically."
  • Lesions Affecting S2-S4 Segments (LMN Bladder): No conscious sensation, no voluntary control, and no bladder emptying reflexes. Leads to "hypotonic (flaccid) bladder, which is distended (overfilled with urine) and is constantly leaking small amounts of urine (incontinence, overflow dribbling)."

F. Bowel Function in SCIs (Neurogenic Bowel)

  • Lesions Above Sacral Cord (UMN Bowel): No awareness of rectal fullness, no voluntary external anal sphincter control. Hypertonic smooth muscle and sphincter. "Tendency for faecal retention and constipation, but rectal stretch can still intermittently elicit reflexive uncontrolled emptying."
  • Sacral SCIs Affecting S2-S4 (LMN Bowel): No awareness, no voluntary control, and loss of emptying reflexes. "Hypotonic bowel with chronic constipation with risk of incontinence due to flaccid paralysis of external anal sphincter."

G. Sexual Function in SCIs

  • Components: Psychogenic (T11-L2 sympathetic) and reflexogenic (S2-S4 parasympathetic).
  • Complete SCIs: Loss of conscious sensation from genitals.
  • SCIs L2-S1: Preserved reflexogenic arousal, incomplete psychogenic.
  • SCIs Above T11: Total loss of psychogenic arousal, preserved reflexogenic.
  • SCIs S2-S4: Loss of reflexogenic arousal, but psychogenic arousal is present.

IV. Stroke

  • Stroke is "acute focal neurological deficit from a vascular disorder." It can be ischaemic (85%) or haemorrhagic (15%).

A. Ischaemic Stroke

  • Causes: Atherothrombotic (large arteries, most common), lacunar (small vessel disease from chronic hypertension/diabetes), cardioembolic (from heart).
  • Clinical Features: Rapid onset (minutes to an hour). Depend on affected area.
    • "Contralateral hemiparesis/hemiplegia and hemihypoaesthesia/hemianaesthesia involving limbs, body and face."
    • Aphasia, dysarthria, dysphagia, apraxia, homonymous hemianopia.
    • Initial flaccidity and decreased reflexes, then UMN signs develop (hypertonia, hyperreflexia, positive Babinski).
    • Headache, seizures, vomiting, depressed consciousness possible.

B. Haemorrhagic Stroke

  • Types: Intracerebral (most common, often due to hypertension), subarachnoid (aneurysm rupture, AVM).
  • Clinical Features: Often sudden onset, severe headache, nausea/vomiting, decreased LOC, focal neurological deficits.

C. Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

  • Definition: "Focal neurological dysfunction due to cerebral ischaemia, not associated with infarction." "Temporary disturbance in cerebral blood flow."
  • Duration: Most symptoms last < 1 hour.
  • Significance: A strong warning sign for future stroke. "Risk of stroke within 3 months of a TIA episode has been reported to be around 20%, with approximately 50% of these strokes occurring within the first 2 days."

D. Diagnosis (Stroke)

  • Clinical: Based on features and neurological exam.
  • Imaging: Crucial to differentiate ischaemic from haemorrhagic stroke.
    • CT: Sensitive for haemorrhagic stroke, less so for ischaemic stroke (especially in first few hours).
    • MRI: More sensitive for ischaemic stroke, especially small infarcts.

V. Seizure Disorders

  • Seizure is "a sudden disorder of cerebral function in form of uncontrolled, transient electrical overactivity in any part of the brain."

A. Pathophysiology

  • Alterations in cell membrane permeability or ion distribution.
  • Abnormal activity of ion channels (channelopathy).
  • Increased excitability of cortical neurons due to neurotransmitter imbalance (e.g., glutamate excess, GABA deficiency).

B. Classification (2017 ILAE)

  • Based on onset, awareness, and manifestations.
  • Focal Onset Seizures: Abnormal electrical activity limited to one part of the brain.

    Awareness:

    • Focal Aware: Person fully aware, may not be able to talk/respond, recalls event.
    • Focal Impaired Awareness: Awareness affected, person confused/disoriented, no memory.

    Manifestations:

    • Motor Onset: Automatism (repeated actions), atonic (loss of muscle tone), tonic (sustained contraction), clonic (rhythmical jerking).
    • Non-motor Onset: Autonomic, behavior arrest, cognitive (impaired thinking, déjà vu/jamais vu, aphasia), emotional, sensory (hallucinations).
  • Focal to Bilateral Tonic-Clonic Seizure: Starts focally, progresses to generalized tonic-clonic.
  • Generalized Onset Seizures: Abnormal electrical activity involves both hemispheres. Most associated with impaired awareness.

    Motor Onset:

    • Tonic-Clonic Seizure (Grand Mal):
      • Tonic Phase: Sudden tonic contraction, "cry," loss of consciousness (up to 1 min).
      • Clonic Phase: Violent jerking, tongue biting, incontinence (1-5 min).
      • Post-ictal Phase: Deep sleep, flaccid muscles, headache, confusion, muscle aches.
    • Myoclonic Seizure: Brief symmetric jerking of extremities/neck.
    • Tonic Seizure: Sustained whole-body contraction, "drop attack" if standing.
    • Atonic Seizure: Sudden loss of postural tone, "drop attack" headfirst.

    Non-motor Onset (Absence Seizure):

    • "Brief staring spells that usually last for few seconds." Often seen in children, associated with "3Hz spike-and-wave activity" on EEG.

C. Diagnosis (Seizure Disorders)

  • Clinical history, physical exam, neurological exam.
  • EEG (Electroencephalography): Records brain electrical activity.
  • Brain Imaging (CT/MRI): To identify structural causes.

D. Treatment (Seizure Disorders)

  • Anticonvulsants: Mechanism includes inhibiting sodium/calcium channels, enhancing GABA inhibition, inhibiting glutamate release. Examples: diazepam, ethosuximide, gabapentin, lamotrigine, phenytoin, topiramate, valproate.
  • Medicinal Cannabis: "Some evidence that medicinal cannabis can help control some unresponsive forms of epilepsy," specifically cannabidiol (CBD).

VI. Parkinson’s Syndrome

  • Parkinson’s syndrome (parkinsonism) is a neurological syndrome characterized by "tremor, slowness of movement, muscle rigidity and postural instability."

A. Types

  • Primary (Idiopathic) Parkinsonism: Parkinson's Disease (PD) – most common type (80% of cases).
  • Secondary Parkinson's Syndrome: Due to structural brain damage (stroke, tumor), medications (dopamine receptor blockers), or other neurodegenerative disorders.

B. Parkinson's Disease (PD)

  • Pathology: "Progressive degenerative disease of the basal nuclei in the CNS." "Marked degeneration of the dopaminergic nigro-striatal tract leading to impaired function of basal nuclei in motor control."
  • Hallmark: Presence of abnormal cytoplasmic deposits called Lewy bodies (in cell body) and Lewy neurites (in axons), composed of α-synuclein protein.
  • Epidemiology: Typically older individuals (>60 years), slightly more common in men. Majority of cases are sporadic (not genetic), but 10% have a genetic cause.
  • Pathophysiology of Symptoms: Increased inhibition in the motor thalamus, leading to decreased excitatory output to motor cortex, manifesting as bradykinesia/hypokinesia. Rigidity and tremor pathophysiology less clear.

C. Clinical Manifestations of Parkinsonism

  • Tremor: "Maximal at rest, diminishes during voluntary activity and is absent during sleep." Often "pill-rolling tremor" in hands (very specific to PD, found in 80%).
  • Muscle Rigidity: "Increased muscle tone to palpation at rest and increased resistance to passive movement." Can be "cogwheel" rigidity.
  • Bradykinesia/Hypokinesia: Slowness of voluntary movement. "Difficulty initiating movements, diminished amplitude of movements, and slowness in completing movements."
  • Postural Instability: Stooped posture, impaired balance, increased fall risk.
  • Gait: "Shuffling gait," small steps, "festination" (increasing speed), difficulty turning. May "freeze in place."
  • Mask-like face (hypomimia): Diminished blinking, mouth open.
  • Soft/monotonic speech, swallowing impairment.
  • Insomnia, fragmented sleep, daytime somnolence.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Greasy skin, constipation, urinary retention, orthostatic hypotension.
  • Cognitive issues: Depression, executive dysfunction, memory recall impairments, dementia (in time).
  • Key Distinction: Sensory perception, muscle strength, and reflexes are typically normal.

D. Diagnosis (Parkinson's Syndrome)

  • Largely clinical.
  • Brain imaging (CT/MRI) to rule out other causes.
  • 18F-DOPA PET imaging / DaT scan (SPECT): Show reduced dopamine function/transporter uptake in basal nuclei.

E. Treatment (Parkinson's Syndrome)

  • Pharmacological: Symptomatic, enhances dopamine activity.
    • Levodopa (dopamine precursor, often with peripheral dopa-decarboxylase inhibitors).
    • Dopamine agonists.
    • Drugs that prolong dopamine effects (e.g., MAO-B inhibitors).
  • Surgical: Deep brain stimulation (DBS) for selected patients.

VII. Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

  • MS is "a chronic inflammatory, demyelinating, and neurodegenerative disease of the central nervous system." It is an "autoimmune disease."

A. Pathology

  • Characterized by "plaques (lesions)" in the CNS.
  • "Loss of myelin (demyelination) accompanied by destruction of oligodendrocytes." Axonal damage and neuronal loss also occur.
  • Lesions are "disseminated in time and space."

B. Epidemiology

  • Prevalence: ~1 per 1,000.
  • Age of Onset: Typically 20-40 years.
  • Gender: "Women being affected twice as frequently as men."

C. Types of MS

  • Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS): Most common (85-90%). "Periods of new or worsening neurological symptoms (relapses) followed by periods of partial or complete recovery (remissions)."
  • Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): Develops from RRMS. "Progressive worsening of neurological function between relapses."
  • Primary Progressive MS (PPMS): Steady progression from the onset.

D. Clinical Manifestations

  • "Almost any neurological symptom can appear with the disease."
  • Muscle weakness, spasticity, tremor.
  • Visual disturbances (optic neuritis, diplopia, scotomas, blurring).
  • Fatigue (very common and troubling).
  • Vertigo, difficulty walking, impaired balance, ataxia.
  • Dysarthria, dysphagia.
  • Urinary and faecal incontinence.
  • Cognitive symptoms (memory impairment, executive dysfunction, concentration issues).
  • Psychological manifestations (depression, anxiety, apathy, emotional lability).
  • Key Distinction: Symptoms are "disseminated in time and space."

E. Diagnosis (MS)

  • Clinical Features: Suspected when "two or more relapses have occurred."
  • MRI with Gadolinium Enhancement: "Most sensitive technique for imaging plaques."
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF): Elevated lymphocytes and proteins (especially IgG on electrophoresis).
  • Evoked Potentials: Measures slowed electrical conduction (e.g., visual evoked potentials).

F. Treatment (MS)

  • Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs): "Immunomodulators that use different strategies to reduce immune attack on myelin." Aim to reduce MRI lesion activity and prevent secondary progressive MS. More effective in RRMS.
  • Glucocorticoids: Reduce severity/duration of relapses, but don't alter disease course.
  • Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS): Quantifies disability in MS (0-10 scale).

G. Prognosis (MS)

  • MS is generally "not a fatal disease," with most patients having a normal or near-normal lifespan, but with reduced quality of life.

VIII. Dementia Syndromes

  • Dementia is a "chronic deterioration of intellectual functions and cognitive skills, which is severe enough to interfere with ability to perform activities of daily living." Deterioration is "beyond what might be expected from normal aging."

A. Epidemiology

  • Mostly a disease of the elderly: "> 15% of persons > 65 years old, and as many as 40% of persons > 80 years old."

B. Core Features

  • Memory impairment.
  • One or more of:
    • Aphasia (language disturbance).
    • Apraxia (impaired ability to carry out complex motor activities).
    • Agnosia (failure to recognize objects/people despite intact sensation).
    • Disturbance in executive functioning (planning, problem-solving).

C. Causes

  • Metabolic: Chronic drug/alcohol abuse, hypothyroidism, vitamin B deficiency.
  • Structural: Alzheimer's, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, Parkinson's disease dementia, brain trauma, tumors, MS.
  • Infective: Late-stage AIDS.

D. Alzheimer’s Disease (AD)

  • Most Common Dementia: Responsible for ~70% of cases. Prevalence increasing.
  • Epidemiology: >95% are over 65. Majority sporadic.
  • Genetics:
    • Risk Genes: APOE e4 (increases risk).
    • Deterministic Genes: APP, PSEN1, PSEN2 (guarantee development, rare, early-onset).
  • Pathology:
    • Amyloid Plaques: Extracellular deposits of β-amyloid protein.
    • Neurofibrillary Tangles: Intracellular aggregates of hyperphosphorylated tau protein.
    • Neurodegeneration: Neuronal loss, brain atrophy (hippocampus and cerebral cortex first).
  • Clinical Progression:
    • Early: Memory loss (especially recent), difficulty learning, mild anomia (word-finding).
    • Moderate: Global cognitive impairment, disorientation, behavioral issues, personality changes, motor deficits develop later.
    • Late: Profound loss of cognitive function, mute, incontinent, bedridden.
  • Diagnosis: Primarily clinical. MRI/CT for brain atrophy and to rule out other causes. PET imaging (amyloid or tau scans) for specific pathological changes.
  • Treatment: Symptomatic (cholinesterase inhibitors, memantine). No cure, but can slow decline.

E. Other Dementias

  • Vascular Dementia: Third most common (~10%). Due to neuronal loss from reduced blood supply (e.g., small strokes, chronic ischemia). Often co-occurs with AD.
  • Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB): Second most common (~15%). Lewy bodies in cortical neurons. Key features: "fluctuating cognition, recurrent visual hallucinations, and spontaneous parkinsonism."
  • Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD): Heterogeneous group. Often characterized by "early changes in personality and behaviour and/or language impairment." Memory problems are less prominent initially.

IX. Motor Neuron Disease (MND)

  • MND refers to "a group of progressive neurological disorders characterised by degeneration and death of motor neurons." Affects UMNs, LMNs, or both.

A. Types

  • Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS): Both UMN and LMN degeneration (most common, 70% of cases).
  • Primary Lateral Sclerosis (PLS): UMN degeneration.
  • Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA): LMN degeneration.
  • Progressive Muscular Atrophy (PMA): LMN degeneration.
  • Progressive Bulbar Palsy (PBP): LMN degeneration affecting bulbar muscles.
  • Pseudobulbar Palsy: UMN degeneration affecting bulbar muscles.

B. Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

  • Lou Gehrig’s disease.
  • Pathology: Degeneration of both UMNs (cerebral cortex/brainstem) and LMNs (brainstem/spinal cord), with LMNs affected more.
  • Genetics: Majority sporadic, ~10% genetic (e.g., C9orf72, SOD1 mutations).
  • Main Features:
    • "Combination of flaccid and spastic paresis/paralysis, together with progressive muscle atrophy."
    • Positive Babinski reflex.
    • Anxiety and depression common.
    • "No mental/cognitive, sensory or autonomic symptoms." "Normal intellectual functions are sustained until death."
  • Onset: Generally slow. Initial signs often muscle weakness in an arm or leg, distally and asymmetrically. Dysarthria and dysphagia in ~30%.
  • Prognosis: Progressive. Respiratory failure is the most common cause of death (median survival 3-5 years from symptom onset).
  • Treatment: Riluzole (glutamate inhibitor) and edaravone (free radical scavenger) modestly slow progression. Symptomatic management for muscle cramps, spasticity, dysphagia, dysarthria.
